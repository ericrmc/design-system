# A House Decision in Five Moves

*For the two of you, when there's a small-to-medium decision about the house on the table — shelving, a patch of garden, a small piece of furniture. Roughly a few hundred dollars, medium-term impact on how the place looks or works. One of you brings it up; the other joins in.*

*These are meant to be done in order, but the conversation doesn't have to happen all at once. You can step away after Move 3 to check a price or a measurement and come back.*

---

## 1. Name the decision

Say what you're deciding — one sentence is enough.

> *Shelving for the lounge, around $500, before the end of the month.*

If you're not sure it's even worth a real conversation, say that too. Sometimes the answer is "let's just do it." Sometimes naming the decision out loud is the only move you need.

---

## 2. Say what each of you cares about

Before options, each of you says what you want this decision to *do* for you. Not *which thing* — what the thing should feel like, work like, or avoid.

> *I want the lounge to feel like a room we read in, not a room we store things in.*

> *I want it to be easy to keep tidy — less dusting, not more.*

> *I don't care much about how it looks, as long as it doesn't make extra work.*

One sentence each is enough. Two if one sentence won't do it. If something matters to you and you haven't said it yet, this is the place.

---

## 3. Narrow to real options

Put two or three real options on the table — the ones you'd actually buy or build, not every possibility. If one of you is already leaning toward something, it goes in the list and the others join it.

If you need to check measurements, prices, or availability before you can really choose, this is the right moment to pause. Come back to Move 4 when the missing facts are in.

---

## 4. Look for the workable yes

Pick the one both of you can live with.

Ask, plainly: **who cares more about this one?**

If one of you cares much more and the other can genuinely live with the caring-more person's preferred option, that usually settles it. Naming it this way is honest — it's not the caring-more person winning; it's the caring-less person granting.

One clause matters, though. **Caring more doesn't automatically beat ongoing burden.** If the caring-more option means the other person pays more, does more of the work, maintains it, or will be mildly annoyed by it every day — that weighs heavily, even when their aesthetic vote was quieter.

If you both care and you disagree, you either keep talking or sleep on it. No other machinery.

---

## 5. Close it, or name what's next

Someone says it out loud:

> *We've decided — we're going with the bookshelves, up to $500, ordering this weekend.*

The other confirms.

If you're not ready to close, say that too, and name when you'll come back to it — a specific day, not "soon."

---

*A few weeks in, you can ask each other: is it doing what we wanted? If yes, good. If no, you haven't failed — you've just learned something about the next one.*

---

## Not this

- Not a scoring matrix. If you want to make a spreadsheet, go ahead, but this isn't one.
- Not a written case. The one-line close is all the writing this needs.
- Not a third-party call. Look up facts, not votes.
- Not a cool-off rule. Sleep on it if you want to; don't if you don't.
- Not built for big decisions. This is for shelving, pavers, small furniture — things in the few-hundred-dollars range. Renovations, vehicles, and life choices deserve more than five moves.
