# Morning Unfurl

*A ~4-minute lower-back wake-up sequence for a stiff lower back, first thing in the morning. One soft mat. No existing routine assumed. No moves ruled out by the practitioner. Designed to be tried once or twice on waking and reported back on.*

---

**Position to start:** supine on the mat. Knees bent, feet flat, about hip-width apart. Arms alongside the body, palms up or down — whichever settles first. Head on the mat.

---

## The sequence (approximately 4 minutes)

**1. Settling breath** — 4 slow breaths, ~20 seconds.

Lie still. Notice where the low back touches the mat and where it doesn't. Don't adjust; just notice. Four slow breaths through the nose. On each exhale, let the low back soften *toward* the mat — without pressing.

*Why this first:* before the low back is asked to move, wake the body's sense of where the low back currently is.

**2. Small pelvic tilts** — 8 repetitions, ~30 seconds.

Still supine, knees bent.
- Inhale: tiny anterior tilt — the low back lifts a fingertip off the mat.
- Exhale: tiny posterior tilt — the low back flattens toward the mat.

Range is a whisper, not a stretch. Tempo matches the breath: roughly 3 counts in, 3 counts out.

*Why this shape:* wake the lumbar–pelvic articulation at micro-range before asking for more.

**3. Knees side-to-side** — 6 slow sweeps (3 each side), ~30 seconds.

Bring the feet a little closer together. Let both knees fall slowly to the right; pause at the comfortable edge (not forcing). Return to center. Let both knees fall slowly to the left; pause. Return.

Breath-led. Unhurried.

*Why this shape:* oscillatory rotation through the low back. Not end-range — the point is a soft sweep, not a maximum twist.

**4. Knee-to-chest, alternating** — 3 breaths each leg, ~40 seconds.

Bring the right knee toward the chest. Interlace hands under the knee, or hold around the shin (whichever the hands prefer). Hold for 3 slow breaths. On each exhale, let the knee come a little closer — without pulling hard.

Release. Bring the left knee to the chest. Hold 3 breaths.

*Why this shape:* gentle lumbar flexion via the hip, not via the spine. One leg at a time (not both) keeps pelvic balance and asks less of a cold low back.

**5. Roll up to hands and knees** — transition, ~10 seconds.

Roll onto one side. Press up to hands and knees — hands under shoulders, knees under hips.

**6. Cat-cow** — 5 slow cycles, ~50 seconds.

- Exhale: round the spine (cat). Pelvis tucks. Low back domes up toward the ceiling. Head drops gently.
- Inhale: arch the spine (cow). Pelvis tilts forward. Chest opens forward. Head lifts gently.

Slow. Breath-led. Not fishing for end-range at either end — the aim is fluid articulation *through* neutral.

**7. Thread-the-needle** — 3 breaths each side, ~40 seconds.

From hands and knees: reach the right arm under the left, threading through. The right shoulder and right ear come toward the mat. Hips stay square over the knees.

Rest there for 3 slow breaths. Return. Repeat on the other side.

*Why this shape:* rotation of the thoracic spine, not the lumbar. Hips staying square over knees concentrates the rotation above the pelvis.

**8. Child's pose** — 5 slow breaths, ~30 seconds.

From hands and knees: big toes together. Knees wide or together — whichever the hips prefer today. Sink hips back toward heels. Arms extend forward, or rest alongside the body. Forehead on the mat or on stacked hands.

*Why to close here:* the sequence closes in sustained, passive lumbar flexion. Gravity does the work. Nothing left demanding effort.

---

## What this sequence refuses (and why)

- **No standing forward folds or seated toe-touches.** Cold posterior chain loads the low back in flexion before the lumbar is articulated. Posterior-chain stretches belong later in the day, after movement and warmth have primed the tissues.
- **No bilateral knees-to-chest (both legs together).** More flexion load on a cold low back than alternating. Single-leg preserves pelvic balance and gives each side its own breath window.
- **No crossed-leg twists** (the top leg pulling the bottom knee across the body). That's an end-range rotational stretch; the sequence's rotation stays oscillatory (knees side-to-side; thread-the-needle). End-range lumbar rotation is earned, not demanded cold.
- **No bridges, bird-dogs, or dead-bugs.** Those are activation / strengthening moves. This sequence is articulation-and-warmth, not engagement. Bridges and bird-dogs belong in a pre-workout sequence, not a wake-up one.

## The order matters

The progression is: supine (lowest load on spine) → supine micro-movement → supine hip-led flexion → quadruped articulation → quadruped rotation → sustained flexion close.

That order builds range gradually. Starting with cat-cow or child's pose instead of pelvic tilts would ask the cold low back for more articulation than it's ready to give.

## Report-back prompts (for after trying it once or twice)

1. **Was every move do-able?** If any move felt forced, unreachable, or aggravating — which one, and what happened.
2. **Did it feel like a phrase you could learn, or like reading instructions off a page?** A phrase you could do again without the text; instructions-off-a-page means the text was doing the work, not the body.
3. **Any difference in the low back after vs. before?** Any direction — better, same, worse, different. Granularity is yours.
4. **Any move you'd add, remove, or modify?** Your feedback defines the next version.
