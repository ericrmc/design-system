## Q1

Identity is sufficient *along specific dimensions* and under-developed along others. My answer is **conditional-yes**: name now, but name to honour the dimensions that have matured, not the dimensions still in formation.

**Three identity markers I find most salient.**

First, **the nine-activity kernel as a stable frame.** Read → Assess → Convene → Deliberate → Decide → Produce → Validate → Record → Close has survived eleven sessions, was revised twice (§7 in Session 009, §1 in Session 011) by its own process, and each revision named senses rather than restructured the sequence. That is identity: a frame that improves under its own discipline without losing its shape. A reviewer three years out would recognise the kernel as the methodology's spine.

Second, **the preservation-of-prior-reasoning convention.** v1/v2/v3 files with explicit `supersedes`/`superseded-by` pointers, 62 decisions with rationale and rejected alternatives, per-decision trigger annotations, stance briefs committed verbatim as provenance. This is not a logging habit; it is a constitutive commitment. The methodology treats reasoning as a durable good. Few methodologies do this; fewer do it this rigorously. This is the most distinctive marker.

Third, **the self-hosting cadence demonstrated.** The methodology runs its own process on itself (Sessions 009, 011 revised the kernel via the kernel; Session 012 is deliberating its own name via its own deliberation machinery). Self-hosting is not merely a property claimed — it has been exercised. Combined with the external-artefact track record (two artefacts in `applications/`, one validated), the methodology has shown it produces work *and* evolves itself by the same procedure.

**Markers I find weak or under-developed.**

The **domain validation loop is thin** — two external artefacts, one validated, one pending. The methodology's claim to produce durable external work rests on a sample of one confirmed case. A name that foregrounded "proven practice" or "tested method" would over-claim.

The **scale of deliberation is narrow** — non-Claude participation is present (Gemini, Codex cited), but the methodology has not been exercised by genuinely independent human perspectives at session-scale. A name that foregrounded "council" or "community" would outrun the evidence.

The **vocabulary is still mobile** — terms like *watchpoints*, *stance briefs*, *criterion-package* have emerged but are young. A name derived from one of them risks freezing a term that may still shift.

**Conditional form.** Name now, but choose a name that honours the *kernel-plus-preservation* identity (the mature dimensions) rather than a name that claims domain-proven, community-scaled, or vocabulary-settled status. A name that points at how-the-methodology-holds-its-reasoning is honest; a name that points at what-the-methodology-has-produced is not yet earned.

## Q2

My Steward role is primarily evaluation, so I offer **one candidate** as a reference point, positioned as an alternative to what the Namer may produce.

**Candidate S-1: Sediment.**

(a) The name itself: *Sediment*.

(b) Derivation: from Latin *sedimentum*, "a settling." Sediment is what accumulates in layers over time; each layer is preserved; later layers sit on earlier ones without erasing them; the sequence can be read back. The methodology's preservation-of-prior-reasoning convention — v1/v2/v3 files, superseded-by pointers, stance briefs committed verbatim — is structurally sedimentary. Reasoning settles; later sessions build on it; the record of what was considered remains legible to anyone who reads down through the layers.

(c) Emphasises: the preservation commitment; the accumulation-across-time property; the legibility of past reasoning to later readers. These are the methodology's most distinctive and most mature dimensions (per Q1).

(d) Understates: the active deliberation machinery (sediment sounds passive); the external-artefact production (sediment is not what you hand someone); the nine-activity kernel structure (a sequence, not a stack). These are real omissions.

(e) Collision check: "Sediment" is a common English word used in geology, publishing software, and occasional product names. I am not aware of a methodology, framework, or prominent software tool named *Sediment* in the design/deliberation/methodology space, but I cannot be certain. [external analogue: geology; the word is borrowed from a domain that handles temporally-layered records, which is precisely the analogue that makes it apply here — I flag this as an external import and argue it applies because the methodology's structural behaviour matches the analogue's mechanic, not because the name is available.]

**Why I offer this one rather than several.** The Namer's role will canvass the naming space. The Steward's contribution is to offer a reference that ties a name to the *mature* identity dimensions specifically, so the evaluation in Q3 has a candidate anchored in the Q1 analysis. *Sediment* is not my confident recommendation — it names the preservation dimension at the cost of the deliberation dimension — but it makes the tradeoff visible.

**What I will NOT propose and why.**

I will not propose names foregrounding "kernel" (e.g., *Kernel Method*) — they freeze a part of the methodology rather than its character, and Q6 warns against names that steer future self-presentation too strongly toward one component.

I will not propose names like *Provenance* as a bare name — too generic, too close to existing usage in version-control and data-lineage contexts; high collision risk.

I will not propose compound-craft names (*DeliberationCraft*, *ProvenanceWorks*) — they read as product names, which the methodology is not.

I will not propose acronyms or puns — the brief forbids this, rightly.

## Q3

A name honours a methodology when it *protects* the methodology's integrity while making it legible externally. Criteria, weighted:

**C1. Identity fidelity (weight: high).** Does the name point at dimensions the methodology has actually matured, not dimensions it aspires to? Per Q1, the mature dimensions are the kernel-as-stable-frame and the preservation-of-reasoning commitment. A name pointing at these is honest; a name pointing at "proven practice" or "community wisdom" over-claims.

**C2. Preservation-compatibility (weight: high).** The methodology's deepest commitment is to preserve prior reasoning. A name must not contradict this: a name implying "the definitive method" or "the final word" would fight the methodology's own reversibility-with-discipline. Names that accommodate revision (by being general, or by pointing at the *process* of holding reasoning rather than a *conclusion*) are preferred.

**C3. Non-ossification (weight: high).** Q6 warns: a name steers future self-presentation. A name that accentuates one component (kernel, provenance, perspectives) risks freezing the methodology's attention there. Criterion: prefer names that do not pre-commit to which component is primary, OR that commit only to the most mature dimension where ossification is the intended outcome.

**C4. External legibility (weight: medium-high).** A first-time reader can use the name in a sentence without sounding strange. "I'm using *Sediment* to work through this decision" — passes. "I'm using *the Methodology* to work through this decision" — fails; requires immediate explanation.

**C5. Collision avoidance (weight: medium).** A name confusable with an existing framework, tool, or book creates ongoing disambiguation cost. Not fatal for obscure collisions; fatal for prominent ones.

**C6. Non-pretentiousness (weight: medium).** The methodology is eleven sessions old with two external artefacts. Names implying grandeur (*The Grand Method*, *Opus*) are disqualified. Names implying modest craft are preferred.

**C7. Translatability (weight: low-medium).** Not a blocker, but if the name embarrasses in a language the author can check, reconsider.

**Tradeoffs.** C1 (identity fidelity) can conflict with C3 (non-ossification): a name maximally faithful to one mature dimension (e.g., preservation) ossifies that dimension. My resolution: where a dimension is *deeply* mature and *constitutively* central, ossification there is acceptable — you are naming what is already true. Where maturity is partial, favour non-ossification.

**Applying to Candidate S-1 (*Sediment*):**

- C1: strong. Points at preservation, the most mature dimension.
- C2: strong. Sediment accommodates new layers without erasing old; the mechanic of the name matches the mechanic of the methodology.
- C3: mixed. Ossifies preservation over deliberation. I judged in Q1 that preservation is constitutively central enough to accept this ossification; another Steward might weigh this differently.
- C4: medium. Legible but slightly odd in a sentence; requires one-sentence explanation.
- C5: likely clear in the methodology space; common word risks low-grade confusion.
- C6: strong. Modest, craft-aligned.
- C7: plain Latin root, translates without embarrassment in European languages; I cannot check all.

**Ranked evaluation (of what I have, not of candidates I haven't seen).** *Sediment* passes C1, C2, C6, C7 cleanly; acceptably on C4, C5; fails partially on C3. I rate it **viable with a caveat** — the caveat being that a Namer may produce a name that holds C1/C2 without ossifying a single dimension. If such a name exists, prefer it. If it does not, *Sediment* is defensible.

## Q4

**Preferred placement: `methodology-kernel.md` title and frontmatter.**

The name belongs where the methodology's load-bearing identity lives. The kernel is the most load-bearing artefact — its nine activities are the spine, it has been revised twice under its own process, and every session's structure is the kernel's structure. If the name is real, it titles the kernel: *"The [Name] Kernel."* If the name cannot survive being the kernel's title, the name is not ready.

Concrete form: the kernel file's title line becomes *"The Sediment Kernel"* (or whatever name wins); frontmatter adds a `methodology_name` field; Section 0 (if present) or a new brief Section 0 notes the name and points at the identity artefact recording its origin.

This placement implies a kernel v-bump (v_current → v_next) and triggers D-023 (kernel revisions require required-trigger multi-agent deliberation with non-Claude participation — which Session 012 already is). Supersession pointers preserved per convention.

**Supplementary placement: PROMPT.md first-paragraph self-reference.**

PROMPT.md is where each session begins. A single line — *"This workspace develops [Name], a methodology that..."* — makes the name present at the entry point. This is medium gravity; it implies a PROMPT.md revision but not a spec v-bump (PROMPT.md's revision discipline is lighter than the kernel's, per what I recall of the workspace).

**Supplementary placement: a small identity artefact.**

`specifications/identity.md` (or similarly named) records: the name, its derivation, the session that named it, the reasoning preserved (pointer to Session 012 provenance), and the condition under which the name could be revised. This is lightweight; it makes the naming itself reviewable and reversible-with-discipline — honouring the methodology's own conventions.

**Alternatives I reject.**

*SESSION-LOG.md header only.* The session log is a ledger; a name living only in the ledger is peripheral. Rejected — doesn't match the name's weight.

*No canonical placement; external references only.* This is the position that says "we use the name when talking to outsiders but keep internal artefacts referring to 'the methodology.'" Rejected — it creates a two-register system in which the methodology is one thing to itself and another thing to the outside. That is exactly the asymmetry Session 009's Validate and Session 011's Read revisions were designed to make honest (workspace/domain senses named, not elided). A split-identity name would contradict that work.

**Tension I record.** Placing the name in the kernel title ossifies the name into the most load-bearing artefact. If the name is later revised, the kernel title is revised with it — which is acceptable under preservation conventions but costly. A Steward committed to reversibility might prefer PROMPT.md + identity-artefact as the primary placement and leave the kernel title untouched. I weigh kernel-title higher because a methodology whose name is not in its kernel is not really named; it is labelled. But I record the tension.

## Q5

The refuse-to-name case at its strongest.

Session 001's Skeptic and Minimalist positions have not been *falsified* by eleven sessions — they have been *deferred*. What would falsify them?

- The Minimalist said naming now is premature; the methodology barely exists. Falsification would require: the methodology exists substantively. I argued in Q1 it does, along specific dimensions. But "exists substantively" is not the same as "has stabilised into what it will be." Eleven sessions is short. Kernel revisions are still happening (§1 last session). Vocabulary is still mobile. The Minimalist's "too early" has been *partially* addressed, not answered.
- The Skeptic said a name creates attachment — once named, the methodology becomes an identity to defend. Falsification would require: evidence the methodology resists defensive attachment when named. We have no such evidence because it has not been named. The Skeptic's claim is intact.
- The Session 001 group specifically noted that "even a 'provisional' name tends to stick." Eleven sessions have produced no counter-evidence to this claim either.

**Integrity concern.** The Steward must also consider: does *not-naming* harm the methodology's integrity? Continued "the methodology" formulation does two things to first-time readers: it signals modesty (the methodology does not claim more than it has earned), and it creates friction (readers must construct a referent each time). The first is a virtue; the second is a tax. The tax is currently small because the workspace is small. As `applications/` grows, the tax grows: external artefacts referring back to "the methodology that produced this guide" sound awkward in ways that will increasingly hurt.

**But.** The Skeptic's strongest form is: *naming is a commitment; identity accumulation is a fact; the two are not the same.* The methodology has accumulated identity. A name is a separate act: it commits the methodology to be the kind of thing that has this name. That commitment is not compelled by the identity accumulation. One can preserve the accumulated identity without naming it, at the cost of the friction tax above.

**What evidence would make naming clearly right rather than defensibly right?**

- A third external artefact, domain-validated, would show the methodology produces work reliably — grounding a name in proven practice rather than incipient practice.
- Adoption by an external practitioner (someone who is not the author) would force a name by external pressure, making the decision less about internal preference and more about external necessity.
- A stabilised vocabulary — specifically, terms like *watchpoints*, *stance briefs*, *triggers* holding unchanged for three or more sessions — would show the methodology's self-description has settled enough to name.

**Form of decline, if Session 012 refuses.** OI-001 remains open, updated with: (i) Session 012's assessment of identity markers; (ii) the specific conditions under which naming would become clearly right (third domain-validated artefact; external adopter; vocabulary stability); (iii) the cost being paid meanwhile (the friction tax on external references). The question is not retired — it is made more specific.

**Why I reject the refuse-to-name case despite finding it strong.** The Skeptic's claim about defensive attachment is real but hypothetical; the friction-tax is real and present. The Minimalist's "too early" is partially answered by eleven sessions of kernel stability and preservation discipline. The Skeptic's "provisional names stick" argument is actually a reason to choose a *non-provisional* name now with care, rather than to keep deferring. But I hold this rejection with low confidence; a perspective weighting the Skeptic higher than I do would decline naming, and that would be a defensible choice.

## Q6

Naming changes *how the methodology is referenced* primarily, and *what the methodology is* secondarily — but the secondary change is real and must be honestly faced.

**One concrete way *Sediment* would steer future self-presentation:** it would accentuate the preservation-of-prior-reasoning commitment and the accumulation-across-sessions property. Future sessions would feel a pull toward making the sedimentary metaphor explicit — describing revisions as "layers," describing old versions as "strata," possibly even reshaping `SESSION-LOG.md` or the v1/v2/v3 file conventions to lean into the metaphor. This is a real steering effect. It would protect the preservation commitment by making it the methodology's self-signature, which is an *intended* protection (per Q1, I argued the mature dimensions deserve to be named into).

**What it would protect specifically:** it would make it harder for a future session to casually weaken the preservation discipline. If the methodology is *Sediment*, a session that proposed "let's stop keeping v1 files when we write v2, it's clutter" would face an immediate incongruence: the name itself would be an argument against. That is a protection function — the name acting as a brake on drift.

**One concrete way *Sediment* would NOT privilege:** it would not foreground deliberation-under-divergence, the forcing of multiple independent perspectives per deliberation. Future sessions remain free to strengthen, formalise, or restructure the perspective-shape rules without the name pulling against them. The perspective machinery (D-016, D-023, trigger coverage, stance briefs) stays available as an evolvable surface. A methodology named *Council* or *Chorus* would privilege perspectives and make perspective-machinery evolution feel load-bearing to the name; *Sediment* leaves it alone.

**Tension recorded.** The protection-versus-steering duality is inescapable. A name that protects one dimension by accentuating it cannot simultaneously leave that dimension fully neutral. The Steward's judgment is: accept accentuation where the dimension is constitutively central and already mature (preservation qualifies); avoid accentuating dimensions still in formation (deliberation-scale, external-validation track record). *Sediment* satisfies this test; it accentuates what is safe to accentuate.

**If refusing to name (per Q5),** staying unnamed steers the methodology toward continued modesty and away from premature self-definition, at the cost of the friction tax on external references. Staying unnamed also preserves a genuine optionality: the methodology can become *any* kind of thing its next external applications reveal it to be, without a name quietly biasing which direction feels coherent.

**Net Steward position across Q1–Q6.** Identity is sufficient along the kernel-stability and preservation dimensions; name to honour those. *Sediment* is a viable candidate anchored in those dimensions; the Namer may produce stronger options. Place the name in the kernel title with supplementary placement in PROMPT.md and a small identity artefact. The refuse-to-name case is strong but loses narrowly to the friction tax and the specificity of what eleven sessions have stabilised. A name will steer future self-presentation toward what it accentuates; choose a name whose accentuation is a protection, not an ambition.
