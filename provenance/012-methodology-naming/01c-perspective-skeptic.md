## Q1

**Answer: No. The identity threshold is not met.**

The brief frames this question with a tell: Session 012's primary work "bounced" (the Session 010 validation receipt hasn't arrived), and naming was selected as the longest-deferred open issue. This is not a neutral condition for assessing identity. A session searching for load-bearing work is disposed to find the work it has available load-bearing. The G/O/K/S package was built precisely to resist this failure mode. It should be applied to this question before the identity markers are.

Let me take the strongest form of the markers-are-sufficient case and then dismantle it.

The three markers most often cited for "enough identity":

1. **Nine activities as a stable frame.** Stable across eleven sessions, yes. But the kernel itself was revised in Sessions 009 and 011 to introduce dual senses (Workspace/Domain) for §7 and §1. That's two of the nine activities renovated within the last three sessions. The frame is stable as a skeleton and actively mutating as content. A name committed now binds to a skeleton whose joints are still being repositioned.

2. **62 decisions with provenance.** Accumulation, not maturation. Decision count scales with session count mechanically — it measures throughput, not convergence. How many of D-001 through D-062 have been tested against actual revision pressure from external use? The honest answer is: very few. Most were stress-tested only by subsequent internal work, which is the same workspace reasoning with itself.

3. **Two external artefacts, one validated.** n=1. One artefact positively validated by its intended user (the lower-back sequence). The household-decision guide is pending. In any other domain, claiming mature identity from a single confirmed external success would be laughed out of the room. It is a successful pilot. It is not a track record.

**Under-developed markers — reasons naming would be premature:**

- **Adoption.** Zero named practitioners outside this workspace. The methodology has never survived contact with someone who did not build it.
- **Revision-pressure from external use.** The lower-back artefact was validated; validation is not the same as returning revision pressure into the kernel. We do not yet know which kernel activities will survive external pressure and which will need renovation.
- **Cross-domain generalisation.** The two artefacts are both "personal micro-guides" (bodily routine; household decision). The methodology's external competence is currently demonstrated in one narrow mode. A name chosen now will be named against that narrow demonstration and may fit poorly as external scope expands.
- **Self-hosting stability.** The methodology is self-hosting (§1 of the brief). A self-hosting system that has revised its own kernel in 2 of its last 3 sessions is not stable — it is successfully iterating. Those are different states. Names belong to the former.

**Rejected alternatives to this answer:** (a) "Yes — enough has accumulated." Answered above; accumulation ≠ identity. (b) "Conditional on external validation of artefact-2 and one third artefact in a different mode." I considered this and rejected it because it frames naming as a near-term reward for modest additional evidence, which is the attachment failure mode dressed as discipline. The right answer is no, under the G/O/K/S test the naming work fails K (an external reader would not flag missing-name as a weakness of current specs) and fails O (naming does not narrow any external action's decision-space — the next external artefact can ship unnamed exactly as the prior two did).

## Q2

I engage Q2 under protest and to serve the deliberation, not to co-produce a ranked list. My position is that generating candidates is already a mild commitment — it creates a discussion surface on which "whether to name" quietly gives way to "which name is best."

Two failure modes that happen *because* the methodology is named, less likely under "the methodology":

- **Identity-defence.** Once named, criticism of the methodology reads as criticism of the named thing. Revising the kernel is now revising "[Name]'s" kernel — the name acquires stakeholders (even if those stakeholders are just the sessions that chose it). Unnamed, the methodology is whatever its current specifications say; there is no identity layer that needs defending above the specs.
- **Scope creep through name-fit.** A name emphasises certain features (see Q6). Future sessions face pressure to foreground those features to "live up to" the name, or to suppress developments that don't fit. Unnamed, new capabilities land where they land.

If pressed — and only to show the candidates make things worse, not to endorse any — here are three I will examine:

**Candidate A: Kernel Method**
- Derivation: the methodology's central specification is called the kernel. Internal vocabulary.
- Emphasises: the nine-activity structure.
- Hides: provenance, preservation of dissent, self-hosting.
- Collision: "kernel method" is a well-established term in machine learning (support vector machines, kernel trick). External collision is severe. Practitioners would have to disambiguate constantly. Rejected on collision grounds even if naming were warranted.

**Candidate B: Provenance**
- Derivation: the methodology's distinctive discipline is preservation of reasoning.
- Emphasises: the archival, reasoning-preserving character.
- Hides: the nine-activity operational frame; external artefact output. A reader hearing "Provenance" would expect a system for tracking data/model lineage — which is exactly what the word denotes in computing and archival practice. [external analogue: W3C PROV, data-provenance literature] — severe collision.
- Rejected.

**Candidate C: The Nine**
- Derivation: the nine activities.
- Emphasises: the kernel's operational structure.
- Hides: everything else. Also pre-commits the methodology to exactly nine — a change to ten or eight would break the name. This is precisely the ossification concern.
- Collision: numeric names are common in fitness programs, mystical frameworks (the Enneagram's nine types [external analogue]), and brand-speak. Embarrassing at a peer table.
- Rejected.

**On the 2-word-or-fewer preference and the no-puns/no-acronyms constraint:** these constraints exist because the brief's authors already sense the failure modes of bad naming. That sensing is correct. The honest extension of it is: most names a session generates under time pressure to do naming-work will fail Q3's criteria. The brief has correctly anticipated that its own Q2 is the weakest question in the deliberation.

**My Q2 conclusion:** no candidate examined improves on "the methodology." Every candidate introduces either a collision, a commitment to a structural feature that may later shift, or a rhetorical emphasis the methodology has not earned with external use.

## Q3

Evaluation criteria are where the rhetorical tilt shows clearest. Let me enumerate and weight honestly.

**Criteria the brief proposes:**
1. External-legibility
2. Memorability
3. Collision-avoidance
4. Translatability
5. Non-pretentiousness
6. Resistance-to-ossification

**Criterion that does not discriminate name-vs-no-name:** Memorability. "The methodology" is instantly remembered because it is self-descriptive. A name must be more memorable than the definite-article-plus-noun, which is a genuinely high bar. Worse, memorability as a criterion subtly reframes the methodology as something that needs to be *marketed* — remembered by whom, for what purpose? The methodology is read by careful agents and by one human operator. Careful readers do not need memorable names; they need precise ones.

**Criterion that most favours not-naming:** Resistance-to-ossification. Every name ossifies. The question is the degree. "The methodology" has maximum resistance-to-ossification because it is a placeholder that explicitly signals its own placeholder status. Any chosen name has less. If this criterion were weighted highly — as the Skeptic position says it should be — no candidate can beat the status quo on this dimension alone.

**Criterion most often invoked dishonestly:** External-legibility. The argument goes "users need something to call it." But users calling the methodology something is different from the methodology carrying a canonical self-reference. External practitioners can and do invent their own references for unnamed things ("that nine-activity kernel Eric uses"). The argument that external legibility *requires* workspace-canonical naming conflates two questions.

**My weighting (Skeptic-honest):**
- Resistance-to-ossification: 40%
- Collision-avoidance: 25%
- Non-pretentiousness: 20%
- External-legibility: 10%
- Translatability: 5%
- Memorability: 0%

**Applying this to Q2 candidates:**

- "the methodology" (no-name baseline): ossification 10/10, collision 10/10, non-pretentious 10/10, legibility 6/10. Weighted: ~9.3/10.
- Kernel Method: ossification 4/10 (commits to kernel-centricity), collision 1/10 (ML), non-pretentious 7/10. Weighted: ~4/10.
- Provenance: ossification 3/10, collision 2/10 (severe domain clash), non-pretentious 8/10. Weighted: ~3.5/10.
- The Nine: ossification 2/10 (pre-commits to exactly nine), collision 5/10, non-pretentious 4/10 (mystical overtones). Weighted: ~3/10.

**Rejected alternative weightings:** I considered a weighting where external-legibility dominates (e.g., 50%). I reject it because external legibility has not been stress-tested — no external practitioner has reported difficulty referring to the methodology. Weighting a criterion heavily when its actual pressure on the system is untested is exactly the confound the methodology's own G/O/K/S package was designed to catch.

**Criterion I would add:** *Under-promise-over-deliver preservation.* A methodology with one validated artefact that carries a confident name over-promises. A methodology with one validated artefact that calls itself "the methodology" under-promises. The second posture is more robust to bad news.

## Q4

Placement is where attachment becomes irreversible. The brief asks where the name lives "canonically." I argue this framing is itself the commitment — there is no neutral placement.

**The three placements the brief considers most load-bearing:**

- **(a) Kernel title/frontmatter.** This binds the name to the primary specification. Every future revision of the kernel carries the name forward. To remove the name, you must revise the kernel (a v-bump under the methodology's preservation discipline) and cite the rename in a new decision. This is the most ossifying placement available and the one a pro-naming perspective will likely favour because it is unambiguous.
- **(b) PROMPT.md first paragraph.** PROMPT.md is the prompt applied each session. A name there becomes a name each perspective encounters at session-start. This is more ossifying than kernel-frontmatter because the name enters the reasoning loop of every future session. The brief underrates how load-bearing PROMPT.md placement is.
- **(c) A dedicated identity file.** Light-touch in appearance, but introduces a new specification whose sole content is a name. This violates single-increment discipline (Constraint D) under the honest reading: adding a new spec to record a name is additional specification work beyond the naming decision. The brief frames this as minimal; it is actually new surface area.

**Rejected placements:**

- **SESSION-LOG.md header.** The session log is append-only chronicle; adding a name to its header makes the name retroactively canonical for all prior sessions, which it wasn't. This is quietly dishonest about when the name was adopted.
- **External references only.** This at least has intellectual honesty — it says "the workspace doesn't need this; external audiences do." But then the name is not really the methodology's own name; it's a label external audiences use. That is fine, and is what happens naturally to unnamed things anyway.

**My preferred placement, conditional on my Q1/Q5 answer being overruled:** option (c), the lightest available, with explicit provisional-status language. A single file `specifications/identity.md` that reads approximately: *"This methodology's working name as of Session NNN is [Name]. The name is provisional and subject to review every N sessions. The workspace primary specifications retain 'the methodology' formulation to preserve reversibility. See D-XXX for the decision rationale and the rejected-alternatives."* This is the placement least likely to ossify.

But my actual preferred placement is **none — no canonical placement, because no name.** See Q5.

**The Skeptic's load-bearing observation on placement:** whichever placement the deliberation chooses reveals its true commitment. A deliberation that genuinely believes naming is reversible chooses (c). A deliberation that is rationalising attachment chooses (a) or (b) while telling itself (c) is "not enough." Watch for that move.

## Q5

**This is my centre. I argue the refuse-to-name case at its strongest.**

Session 001's Skeptic and Minimalist said: names ossify; early naming creates attachment; a provisional name tends to stick. Eleven sessions have passed. The brief invites us to consider whether those arguments have been falsified. They have not. They have been strengthened.

**The sharpest form of the attachment concern after eleven sessions:**

The methodology's distinctive disciplines are: preservation of dissent, revision-over-replacement, evolving-by-revision, self-hosting. Each of these is a *refusal of premature closure*. The kernel has been revised (§7 in Session 009, §1 in Session 011). Specifications are preserved as v1, v2, v3 rather than overwritten. Dissenting perspectives are recorded at their strongest rather than smoothed to consensus. The methodology's whole character is the discipline of not-yet-closing things.

A name is a closure. It says: this thing is sufficiently this-thing to be called something. Every discipline the methodology has cultivated over eleven sessions argues against that closure now.

Further — and this is the sharpest form — **the methodology has been performing its own Skeptic discipline on its own naming question since Session 001.** OI-001 has remained open for eleven sessions. That is not a bug or a deferral. That is the methodology doing exactly what it teaches: preserving a question at its strongest until it is genuinely ready to close. Closing it now because Session 012's primary work didn't arrive is *inverting* the discipline. It is the methodology performing its own bad move.

**What specifically is served by remaining unnamed:**

1. **Self-hosting integrity.** A self-hosting methodology that has a name presents a target for defence. Unnamed, the methodology and its current specifications are the same thing — there is no identity-above-specification.
2. **Revision elasticity.** The kernel has been revised twice recently. More revisions are foreseeable. An unnamed methodology absorbs revision without renaming pressure.
3. **External restraint.** An unnamed methodology does not invite adoption before it is ready. Once named, the methodology becomes easier to cite, share, evangelise — and the project is not yet mature enough to survive enthusiastic adoption well.
4. **Preservation-of-dissent integrity.** Session 001's dissent is in the record. Overruling it when no new evidence has arrived (only more sessions of the same workspace) is bad provenance. The methodology would be breaking its own rule.

**What evidence would have to become true before I withdraw my refusal (falsifiability conditions):**

I commit to the following explicit conditions. Any one of these, honestly met, converts my refusal to support-for-naming:

- **External adoption threshold.** At least one named practitioner outside this workspace uses the methodology for at least three months on their own work, and reports back with pressure that affected at least one kernel revision.
- **Cross-domain artefact threshold.** At least three external artefacts shipped across at least two meaningfully different domains (not two "personal micro-guides"), each with positive domain validation.
- **Kernel-stability threshold.** At least five consecutive sessions with no kernel revision — demonstrating that the nine-activity frame has stabilised under pressure rather than merely between pressures.
- **External naming demand.** An actual external reader or user requests a name because the no-name formulation has blocked them from doing something specific. This has never happened.

None of these conditions is currently met. None is close to met.

**The concrete form of the decline:**

- **Decision record (proposed D-063):** "Session 012 declined to name the methodology. The methodology's distinctive disciplines — revision-over-replacement, preservation of dissent, self-hosting — argue against premature closure. Session 001's Q6 reasoning is re-affirmed with eleven sessions of additional evidence that the methodology is still actively revising its own kernel. The refuse-to-name position carries explicit falsifiability conditions (see below) such that naming becomes re-openable on named evidence."
- **OI-001 after this session:** remains open. Add: "Session 012 re-examined. Refusal sustained. Falsifiability conditions recorded [as above]. Next scheduled re-examination: when any falsifiability condition is met, not by session count."
- **SESSION-LOG entry:** "Session 012: Re-examined OI-001 (methodology naming). Refusal sustained with explicit falsifiability conditions. No spec revisions; no new spec files; single-increment discipline held."

**Rejected alternatives to refusal:**

- **"Name provisionally and revisit."** Session 001 already rejected this, on the reasoning that even provisional names stick. Eleven sessions have not weakened that reasoning.
- **"Name lightly, placement (c) only."** This is provisional-naming with better manners. Same failure mode.
- **"Name but commit to revisit every N sessions."** Makes the methodology's self-presentation depend on a scheduled governance ritual that the methodology has no other instance of. Introduces new machinery for the sole purpose of licensing a name. Net-new surface area for no net-new capability.

**Against my own role — a tension recorded honestly:**

I have one uncomfortable admission. The Historian's Session 001 argument — that every serious methodology has a name — has some force that accumulates over time, not decreases. If the methodology is still unnamed at Session 030, that itself may signal something. I do not think we are there yet; I think Session 012 is far too early; but I note that my refusal is not permanent and the falsifiability conditions above are the honest form of that non-permanence.

## Q6

Naming changes what the methodology *is*, not only how it's referenced. The brief's framing is correct; most perspectives will under-register how strongly this is true.

**What staying unnamed steers the methodology toward:**

- **Emphasis on current-specification-as-identity.** Unnamed, the methodology is defined operationally by what its specifications currently say. Change the specs, change the methodology. There is no layer above the specs to preserve.
- **Low-stakes revision.** Revising an unnamed system feels like clarifying a draft. Revising [Name] feels like revising [Name]. The latter invites heavier deliberation than the revision itself warrants.
- **External humility.** The methodology does not present as a brand. It presents as a working system. That posture fits its actual maturity.

**Interpretive moves available now (unnamed) that become harder once named:**

1. **Treating a future kernel revision as disclosure-of-error rather than identity-update.** Right now, if Session 020 revises the kernel to eight activities or ten, that revision is a specification change and its provenance sits in a decision. Once named — especially if the name emphasises the nine-activity structure — the same revision becomes an "identity update." The provenance load grows. The revision becomes harder to execute honestly.

2. **Spawning an adjacent methodology without schism.** Unnamed, if a future session decides the methodology should fork to handle (say) team-scale deliberation distinct from single-operator work, that fork is just additional specification. Named, the fork is a brand question. "Is the team-scale version [Name]-team, or is it a different methodology?" Naming invents a problem that does not currently exist.

**Suppose, against my Q1/Q5 answer, a name is chosen.** Take the least-bad candidate (I will not endorse one; I will describe effects hypothetically). Any name with operational flavour (kernel-ish, activity-ish) would steer future sessions toward further operational-kernel refinement and away from the methodology's other equally real features (provenance, preservation-of-dissent). Any name with provenance flavour would do the opposite. There is no neutral name. The claim that a "neutral-sounding" name defers the question is, on examination, the claim that the methodology should adopt a name that pretends not to steer. That is worse than steering openly, because it hides the steering.

**One property naming cannot privilege:** adaptability to a domain the methodology has not yet been applied to. Any name picked from current-identity will be picked against the two existing external artefacts (personal micro-guides) and the current nine-activity frame. If the methodology's third external artefact is in a genuinely different domain (e.g., a multi-party process, a software architecture decision, a research protocol) the name will misfit in a way that cannot be predicted from current evidence. Staying unnamed preserves the right to discover that misfit before it is locked in.
