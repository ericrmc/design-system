**12-Minute Micro-Pattern: 1-2-4-All Sprint**

Use this when you want a fast progression from private thinking to shared synthesis without letting the loudest voices set the frame too early. It works well for questions that need judgment, interpretation, or prioritization rather than simple factual recall.

**Question criteria**
Use one clear prompt, stated in a single sentence. Example: “What is the most important shift we should make in the next 30 days, and why?”

**Room setup**
Twelve adults in one room. Ask people to be ready to form pairs, then pairs-of-pairs. Everyone should have something to write with.

### 0:00-0:45 | Whole group | Frame the task
**Facilitator says:**
“I’m going to give you one question. We’ll move from silent individual thinking to pairs, then groups of four, then full-group synthesis. Keep your contributions brief so we can build, not repeat. Your job is not to defend your first idea; it’s to help the room find the strongest shared answer.”

“Here is the question: [state question]. Write it down if helpful.”

### 0:45-2:15 | Individuals | Silent thought
**Time:** 1 minute 30 seconds

**Facilitator says:**
“For the next ninety seconds, work alone and in silence. Write down one answer, one reason, and one phrase you’d want the whole group to hear. Don’t polish it. Capture the core.”

At 1 minute:
“Thirty seconds left. If you have several ideas, circle the one you most want to test with others.”

### 2:15-4:15 | Pairs | Compare and sharpen
**Time:** 2 minutes

**Facilitator says:**
“Turn to one person near you. Each of you gets about forty-five seconds to share your answer and reason. Then use the remaining time to find what overlaps, what differs, and what feels strongest.”

“Your output as a pair is this: one combined answer in a single sentence.”

At 3:30:
“Thirty seconds left. Make sure you have one sentence, not two separate opinions.”

### 4:15-7:15 | Groups of four | Integrate and prioritize
**Time:** 3 minutes

**Facilitator says:**
“Now join another pair to make a group of four. Each pair reads its one-sentence answer. Then, as four, combine them into one sharper statement.”

“Do two things: first, identify the common thread. Second, choose the strongest supporting reason. Your output is one statement and one reason. Pick one spokesperson.”

At 6:15:
“One minute left. Move from discussion to decision. If you can’t agree on every word, agree on the central idea.”

### 7:15-10:45 | Whole group of 12 | Harvest and synthesize
**Time:** 3 minutes 30 seconds

**Facilitator says:**
“We’re back as one group. I’ll hear from each group of four in turn. Spokesperson: give us only your statement and your reason, in twenty seconds or less.”

After each report, the facilitator briefly captures key words on a board or shared note.

After all three groups report:
“Listen to what’s on the board. What themes repeat across all three? What is distinct but important? I’m looking for the shortest possible synthesis that still feels true.”

Invite two or three very short responses:
“One phrase at a time.”
“No speeches.”
“Build on what’s there.”

### 10:45-12:00 | Whole group | Confirm final synthesis
**Time:** 1 minute 15 seconds

**Facilitator says:**
“Here is the synthesis I’m hearing: [state a draft in one or two sentences].”

“Quick check: what, if anything, is missing that changes the meaning?”

Pause briefly, then close:
“If there’s no major correction, this is our group answer: [state final version].”

“If we were to carry one sentence from this conversation into action, it would be this.”

**Facilitation notes**
Keep transitions crisp and visible. Announce time remaining at least once in each segment after the silent round. If energy drops, tighten the instruction rather than expanding the discussion. The pattern works because each round reduces volume and increases quality: many ideas individually, tested in pairs, integrated in fours, then synthesized by the whole.
