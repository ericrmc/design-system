Let yourself arrive on the floor exactly as you are. There is nothing to fix right now, and nothing to perform. Your knees are bent, your feet are flat, and the small book under your head is simply there to let your neck be quieter. For these first few minutes, you are not trying to stretch, strengthen, or improve yourself. You are here to be supported.

Allow the floor to hold your weight. You do not have to hold yourself up. Notice that your back is receiving support, your pelvis is receiving support, your ribs are receiving support, and the back of your head is receiving support through the book. Let that be enough for now.

Take a moment to recognize the habit of effort. If your day has been full of screens, deadlines, and bracing, your system may still be expecting to do something. You do not need to follow that impulse. If you notice the wish to adjust, correct, or help this experience along, simply notice that wish and let it pass. There is no need to move.

Let your jaw be unoccupied. Let your tongue rest in your mouth without arranging it. Let your eyes settle back, as though they could rest away from the work of looking. The skin of your forehead can soften without you making it soften. Your throat does not need to open. Your chest does not need to relax. You are only noticing what is here.

Now begin to notice your contact with the floor more specifically. Feel the weight through your heels. Feel the broadness of your feet meeting the ground. Sense the back of your pelvis, the ribcage, the shoulders, the arms. You are not trying to press any of these places down. You are simply allowing yourself to be met.

Notice your breathing exactly as it is. You do not need to deepen it. You do not need to make it slower. Let the inhale come on its own, and let the exhale leave on its own. If the breath feels shallow, let it be shallow. If it feels uneven, let it be uneven. Trust that your breathing can happen without your management.

As you rest, you may begin to detect areas that seem busy: the lower back gripping a little, the neck doing more than necessary, the shoulders slightly lifted inside, the belly held, the thighs still carrying the day. You do not need to release these places directly. Instead, let your attention include them kindly, without argument. Often the first form of release is simply no longer demanding change.

If thoughts come in, let them come and go in the background. Planning, replaying, evaluating, all of that can continue without needing your participation. Keep returning to the plain facts of support: floor under feet, floor under back, book under head, breath moving by itself.

For the remainder of these first minutes, stay with that. Supported. Still. Unhurried. Let the ground do the work of holding you. Let your muscles receive the message that, for now, nothing is required. You are allowed to rest before anything feels different. You are allowed to be here without improvement. Just this quiet contact, this simple breathing, and this permission to stop.
