Let the floor take your weight. You don't need to hold yourself up anymore. The ground is there, underneath you, and it will do the work of supporting you for as long as you lie here. There is nothing you need to do to be held.

Notice where your head rests on the book. Feel the back of your skull, heavy, resting. You don't need to press down, and you don't need to lift away. The weight of your head is simply there, received by the small platform beneath it.

Let your eyes be soft. They don't need to focus on anything. If they're open, let them take in light without searching. If they're closed, let the darkness behind them be spacious rather than tight. Your eyes don't have to work right now.

Bring your attention to your jaw. Notice whether your teeth are touching, whether your tongue is pressed against the roof of your mouth. You don't need to change anything — just notice. Often the noticing itself allows a small release. The jaw can be heavy. The tongue can rest low and wide in the mouth.

Feel your shoulders against the floor. Notice the places where they make contact and the places where they don't. There may be a gap beneath one shoulder blade, or a sense that one side meets the ground more fully than the other. This is information, not a problem. You're not trying to flatten yourself. You're simply letting the floor be where it is, and letting your body be where it is.

Now your back. The long stretch from the base of your skull down to your pelvis. Let your ribs be heavy. Let your breath move them without effort — you're not breathing deeply, you're not breathing in any particular way. Breath is happening. Air is arriving and leaving. You don't need to manage it.

Feel your pelvis resting on the floor. Your lower back may or may not touch the ground, and either is fine. With your knees bent and your feet flat, the lower back is free to be whatever length it wants to be. You're not trying to press it down. You're not trying to arch it up. It's simply there.

Notice your feet. The soles are in contact with the floor. Your legs are holding your knees up in a soft tent shape, but they don't have to grip. The bones of your legs can rest along their own lines. Let your thighs be heavy. Let your calves be heavy. Let your feet spread a little into the floor.

Your arms. Wherever they are — by your sides, resting on your belly, wide on the floor — let them be heavy too. The hands don't need to do anything. The fingers can be slightly curled, slightly open, whatever shape they fall into when you stop arranging them.

This is the practice. Nothing more. You're here, on the floor, not doing. The tension that lives in you has been working for a long time. Right now, you're giving it permission to stop.
