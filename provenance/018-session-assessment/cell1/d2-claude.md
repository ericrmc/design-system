"Good morning, everyone. Thank you for being here. Before we get into the substance of this retrospective, I want to take a few minutes to set the tone, because how we talk over the next hour matters as much as what we talk about.

This project didn't land where we hoped. Things went wrong. Some of them went quite wrong. We are going to name those things today, clearly and specifically, because that is the only way we learn from them. But I want to be explicit about something up front: we are here to examine the system, not to examine each other.

Every decision that was made in this project was made by a reasonable person, with the information they had, under the pressures they were under, trying to do good work. That is my baseline assumption, and I am asking you to hold it too. When we surface a failure today, the question in the room is never 'who' — it is 'what were the conditions that made this the path of least resistance?' A missed deadline is a signal about estimation, scope, or dependencies. A defect that shipped is a signal about our review process or our tests. A decision that turned out badly is a signal about what we knew and what we couldn't have known at the time.

So here is what I am asking of all of us. Speak plainly about what happened. Use 'we' and 'the process' rather than names, even when you are talking about your own work. If someone names something you were close to, resist the urge to defend — get curious instead. And if you notice blame creeping into the room, including from me, call it out. That is a contribution, not an interruption.

Nothing said here leaves this room attached to a person. Let's find out what actually happened."
