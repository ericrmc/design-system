## Q2. Move 5 expansion (correction C3)

C3 is the one correction where I agree with the user the original artefact has a real gap. Closing a decision and naming who carries it are not the same act. The user named a concrete burden: scheduling, ordering, deciding delivery-or-pickup, being home. The artefact is silent on this and should not be.

The question is shape. Inline, new move, or restructured. I argue for **restructured Move 5** — the same move, rewritten so it handles both closing and naming the owner, with the move's name changed to match. Not a new Move 6. Not a sidebar inline.

Proposed text:

> ## 5. Close it, and say who picks it up
>
> Someone says it out loud:
>
> > *"We've decided — bookshelves, up to $500, ordering this weekend."*
>
> The other confirms. That's the decision closed.
>
> Then — same breath, or next one — say who's picking it up. Not who *owns* it; you both own it. Who's scheduling the order, tracking the delivery, being home when it arrives, chasing up the invoice. It's a real bit of work, and it's easier when one of you has said "I've got this" than when it's left vague and then one of you quietly ends up with it.
>
> The other one can offer to take a piece — "I can be home Thursday" — but the point is that the decision isn't closed until someone has said they'll carry it through.
>
> If you're not ready to close, say that too, and name when you'll come back to it — a specific day, not "soon."

**Why restructured-in-place, not a new Move 6.**

The user did not describe the follow-up as a separate phase of the conversation. They described it as something that needs to be named *when you close* — "to close it, we also need to name the person responsible for following up." That grammar matters. The closing moment and the owner-naming moment are two beats of the same conversational act. A couple, at the end of a twenty-minute talk about shelving, doesn't close, then pause, then open a sixth move. They close, and someone says "I'll handle the order." Putting these in separate numbered moves makes them feel sequential when they are adjacent. It also grows the total count to six for reasons the user did not name, which is exactly the scope creep I am here to resist.

**Why restructured, not inline-as-a-new-paragraph inside the current Move 5.**

Inline would be cheapest and is the Skeptic-natural instinct. I considered and reject it because the move's *name* — "Close it, or name what's next" — now mis-describes what the move does. A move that closes-and-assigns should say so in its heading. Readers skimming the artefact will rely on move headings; leaving the heading alone while cramming assignment into the body produces an artefact whose table of contents lies. That's a small thing but it's a real thing. The rename ("Close it, and say who picks it up") is cheap and preserves individual-addressability: a couple scanning for "where does the owner question live?" finds it in the right place.

**Does naming an owner in the same breath as closing create awkward hierarchy?** Possibly. I addressed it by writing: "Not who *owns* it; you both own it. Who's scheduling the order…" This distinction — ownership of decision vs. execution of followthrough — is the user's own distinction, recast in the artefact's register. It's also load-bearing: without it, the sentence "say who picks it up" reads as though one person becomes junior-partner-on-this-decision. The artefact needs the one qualifying phrase. It does not need a paragraph on it.

**Alternatives considered and rejected:**

- **Inline in current Move 5, no rename.** Rejected above — the heading would mis-describe the move. Would pass Q5's scope-creep test but fails the individually-addressable-moves constraint.
- **New Move 6.** Rejected because it implies sequential conversational beats where there are two beats of one act, and grows the count without user warrant. A Reviser perspective will want Move 6. I think they're wrong.
- **Inline plus an explicit "handoff" diagram or bullet list of sub-tasks.** This is the corporate-drift failure mode. The user named four concrete sub-tasks (schedule, order, delivery-or-pickup, be home); the temptation is to enumerate them as a checklist. I wrote them as prose inside one sentence instead. The artefact loses the governance-list reading the user specifically did not want. This matters — C2 is fresh evidence the user reads corporate drift quickly.

## Q3. Handling replaced items (correction C4)

Here I hold the strongest Skeptic position. **C4 should not produce artefact body text.** The user's own framing — "a few intricacies which vary by domain" — diagnoses this correctly. The disposal/donation/reuse sub-sequence is a furniture-domain thing, not a household-decision-general thing. Adding a "handling replaced items" step or section would embed a furniture-specific scaffold in a protocol designed to span shelving, landscaping, small furniture, and whatever else this couple decides next.

My preferred option: **a single sentence in the opening framing or after-note** acknowledging that domain-specific intricacies exist, without enumerating them.

Proposed text, added to the after-note (or immediately after Move 5):

> *Some decisions carry extra bits that vary by kind — clearing out what's being replaced, coordinating with a tradesperson, timing around seasons. Handle those where they fit; the five moves above are the shape of the conversation, not the whole job.*

That's one sentence. It does the load-bearing work of acknowledging what the user named. It does not enumerate disposal, donation, or reuse. It does not commit the artefact to addressing landscape-scheduling or plumber-coordination in the body.

**Why I reject enumeration:**

- *Option A (general step in the main sequence — add Move 6 or 7).* This is the worst option. It converts a user observation about domain-specificity into a universal step that doesn't apply to most decisions the artefact is designed for. A "handle the old one" step makes no sense for landscaping or a new coffee table if nothing is being replaced. Readers will either skip it (in which case it is dead weight and undermines trust in the sequence) or feel obliged to engage with it (in which case the artefact has grown a false-universal). This is exactly the scope creep the brief warns about and exactly the kind of thing the user did *not* ask for.

- *Option C (domain-sidebar section, "If you're replacing something").* Better than A, worse than my proposal. It commits the artefact to one domain sidebar and implicitly invites further ones ("If you're hiring a tradesperson…", "If it's seasonal…"). The artefact's one-page discipline relies on refusing the sidebar pattern. Let one sidebar in and the next session revising this artefact will add three more. There's no principled stopping point once you've conceded that domain-specific sub-procedures belong in the body.

- *Option B (conditional note within Move 5).* This option is tempting because the handoff and the disposal both live at the "after the decision is closed" end of the conversation. I reject it because Move 5 is already absorbing the C3 revision (close-and-owner). Adding a third concern — "if you're replacing something, also handle the old one" — loads Move 5 beyond what a casual one-page artefact can carry without feeling bloated. Move 5 becomes the move-that-handles-everything, which is a structural failure.

- *Option E (not at all, not even a note).* This is the pure Skeptic maximalist position and I came close to arguing for it. The case: the user themselves called this a domain-specific intricacy; an artefact that doesn't cover domain-specific intricacies is behaving correctly by not covering this one; the couple's judgment handles it without artefact prompting. I reject this option because the user *did* name the concern specifically, and the opening framing already gestures at the artefact's scope ("small-to-medium decision about the house"). A one-sentence acknowledgment that the conversation-shape does not capture the whole job is honest and cheap. Full silence would be a small thing the artefact could do better.

**So my position is: Option D (framing-mention), one sentence, no enumeration.** It is the least change consistent with acknowledging C4 and honours the user's own "varies by domain" framing instead of overruling it.
