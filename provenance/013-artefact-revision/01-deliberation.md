## Q1 — Move 1 revision (C1 and C2)

**Four-way convergence on question-opener framing.** All four perspectives
independently arrived at the same core diagnosis: Move 1 should begin as a
question (not a declarative sentence) and constraints should not be listed up
front.

- [Reviser, Q1]: *"Open it as a question, not a verdict."*
- [Householder, Q1]: *"One of you starts — usually with a question, not an answer."*
- [Skeptic, Q1]: *"Ask the question out loud — one of you starts. It's an opening, not a pitch."*
- [Outsider, Q1]: *"Open with the question, not the whole answer."*

**Four-way convergence on preserving the "let's just do it" paragraph.** All four
preserve the closing paragraph unchanged. None propose touching it.

**Four-way convergence on loosening the constraint-declaration expectation.** All
four add explicit text telling readers they don't need budget/timing/scope up
front.

- [Reviser, Q1]: *"You don't need to know the budget, the timing, or the shape of the answer yet."*
- [Householder, Q1]: *"You don't need to know the budget, the timing, or exactly what you're buying yet."*
- [Skeptic, Q1]: *"You don't need to know the budget or the timing yet."*
- [Outsider, Q1]: *"You do not need to settle the budget, timing, or exact shape of the decision yet."*

**Variance on number of example openings.** Reviser proposes two examples (lounge,
garden); Householder, Skeptic, and Outsider each propose three. The three-example
versions use different specific openers. The user's own feedback gave two example
openers ("Should we talk about the lounge?" / "What should we do about the lounge?").

**Outsider's concrete failure-mode flag** [01d, Q1]: *"A Claude-family failure mode
here is wording like 'surface the decision' or 'check in on the lounge.' Both are
wrong register."* And [01d, Q6]: *"'Should we talk about the lounge?' is not only
a topic label. It is also a light request for attention and timing. It gives the
other person room to say 'not right now.' Claude-family models often flatten that
into a neat naming device and miss the social permission built into the question
form."* No Claude perspective produced the attention/timing observation — it is
Outsider-original and load-bearing for the register choice.

**Reviser's meta-observation on C2** [01a, Q6]: *"The corporate feel comes from the
listing gesture, not the words."* This is a diagnostic refinement of C2 — the
flagged line's failure is syntactic (list-as-sentence) rather than lexical
(corporate vocabulary). The Reviser's Q1 replacement addresses C2 by removing the
list-as-sentence structure entirely, not by casualifying vocabulary.

**Synthesis Q1 position:** Adopt the convergent question-opener framing with
three example openings (majority convergence: Householder + Skeptic + Outsider).
Preserve the "let's just do it" paragraph. Use the Outsider's observation about
social permission / attention / timing to inform example-line selection — openers
that leave room for "not right now." Honour the Reviser's diagnostic refinement
that C2's problem is list-as-sentence, not vocabulary, by not listing constraints
anywhere in the replacement.

**Candidate Move 1 text for synthesis** (drawing Reviser's precision verbs,
Householder's "one of you starts" construction, Outsider's "One line is enough"
permission, and example openings from all three three-example proposers):

```markdown
## 1. Name the decision

One of you opens the conversation — usually with a question, not an answer. One line is enough.

> *"Should we talk about the lounge?"*
>
> *"What should we do about that empty corner of the garden?"*
>
> *"Do we want to sort the shelving out, or leave it for now?"*

You don't need to know the budget, the timing, or exactly what you're buying yet. Some of that only surfaces later, once you've both said what you care about.

If you're not sure it's even worth a real conversation, say that too. Sometimes the answer is "let's just do it." Sometimes naming the decision out loud is the only move you need.
```

The three examples are drawn from across the proposals: the Outsider's variant
of the user's own lounge-question, a garden-strip opener (Reviser+Householder),
and the Outsider's "sort the shelving out, or leave it for now" opener (which
carries the Outsider-original "leave it for now" permission that a Claude
synthesis might flatten).

## Q2 — Move 5 expansion (C3)

**3-of-4 cross-model convergence on restructured-in-place.** Reviser, Skeptic, and
Outsider all argue that the close and the owner-naming are two beats of one
conversational act, and that a new Move 6 would create false ceremony.

- [Reviser, Q2]: *"The user describes the follow-up burden as continuous with the close…The 'also' is the telling word."*
- [Skeptic, Q2]: *"The closing moment and the owner-naming moment are two beats of the same conversational act. A couple, at the end of a twenty-minute talk about shelving, doesn't close, then pause, then open a sixth move."*
- [Outsider, Q2]: *"In real use, couples do not experience 'we've decided' and 'who's actually doing the next bit' as two separate phases. They happen in one breath. A separate Move 6 would create false ceremony."*

This is the session's strongest cross-model convergence — three independent
perspectives (including the non-Claude Outsider) arriving at "restructured in
place" over "new Move 6" with substantially similar reasoning.

**Householder's load-bearing dissent** [01b, Q2]: *"Close-the-decision and
name-the-owner are two different moments, not one, and the artefact has to treat
them that way…when the artefact pretends they do it produces the exact failure
I'm most familiar with: both people leave the conversation believing it's closed,
neither commits to the work, weeks pass."* The Householder's structural argument
is that a separate Move 6 creates the pause in which the awkward owner-naming
conversation actually happens, and that collapsing the beats inherits the
caring-more-equals-owning-execution default from Move 4.

The Householder's dissent is preserved as first-class minority. It is substantively
different from the 3-of-4 majority's rationale, and if the revised artefact's next
use surfaces the failure mode the Householder named (both people believe closed,
neither commits), the dissent is the explicit warrant for re-examining the
restructured-in-place decision.

**Outsider's terminology flag** [01d, Q2]: *"I would not use the word 'owner' in
the artefact, even if the design discussion uses it. 'Owner' is project-management
language. It will read as imported workplace structure. 'Who is carrying the
follow-through' is plainer and more domestic."* No Claude perspective flagged
"owner" as hazardous terminology; Reviser used "one of you takes it on" and
"whoever's following up" (verbal, not nominal); Householder's structural proposal
used "owner" as a design-discussion term but acknowledged enumeration-as-checklist
would drift corporate; Skeptic used "picks it up" and made the "you both own it"
clarification. The Outsider's flag is consistent with Reviser and Skeptic's verbal
avoidance, but names the pattern explicitly, and the Outsider's preferred
replacement ("who is carrying the follow-through") is adopted as the move's title:
**"Close it, and name the follow-through."**

**Title variance across proposals:**

| Perspective | Proposed title |
|---|---|
| Reviser | Close it, and name who's following up |
| Householder | Move 5: Close it / Move 6: Name who's following up |
| Skeptic | Close it, and say who picks it up |
| Outsider | Close it, and name the follow-through |

The Outsider's title is adopted because (a) it avoids the "who" construction that
slightly personalises the role and (b) it uses "follow-through" (Outsider's
"plainer and more domestic" term) rather than "following up" which three
perspectives used but which is slightly more project-management-adjacent.

**Synthesis Q2 position:** Restructure Move 5 in place, retitled to "Close it, and
name the follow-through." Adopt the Outsider's "follow-through" terminology as
the load-bearing noun, avoiding "owner." Handle the mental-burden content by
naming the category (ordering, scheduling, receiving) without enumerating as a
checklist. Include the Skeptic's "you both own it" clarification in a light form
to address the awkward-hierarchy concern. Allow for split follow-through per the
Outsider's two-example demonstration ("I'll order them tonight and sort delivery"
/ "I'll be home Saturday if they need a delivery window"). Preserve the "if you're
not ready to close" paragraph unchanged.

**Candidate Move 5 text for synthesis** (drawing Skeptic's "you both own it"
clarification, Outsider's split follow-through example structure, Reviser's
"not a full plan" scope-discipline line, and Reviser's casual example line
structure):

```markdown
## 5. Close it, and name the follow-through

Someone says it out loud:

> *"Alright — bookshelves, up to $500, let's order them this weekend."*

The other confirms. That's the decision closed.

Then say who's handling what comes next — ordering, scheduling, being home for delivery, whatever the thing needs. It's a real bit of work; you both own the decision, but one of you is carrying the next steps. If it splits naturally, say how.

> *"I'll place the order tonight."*
>
> *"I can be home Saturday for the delivery."*

Not a full plan — just enough that you both know what's been taken on.

If you're not ready to close, say that too, and name when you'll come back to it — a specific day, not "soon."
```

## Q3 — Handling replaced items (C4)

**3-of-4 convergence on close-context placement.** Reviser, Householder, and
Outsider agree the disposal-handling belongs in the close/follow-through move as
a conditional note. Within that agreement, Reviser and Outsider place it in
Move 5 (consistent with their Q2 restructured-in-place position); Householder
places it in Move 6 (consistent with their Q2 new-Move-6 position).

- [Reviser, Q3]: *"A conditional note within Move 5, kept short, placed after the 'if you're not ready to close' paragraph."*
- [Householder, Q3]: *"A conditional note within the new Move 6, not a new standalone move and not a sidebar section."*
- [Outsider, Q3]: *"The replaced-item issue belongs as a conditional note within Move 5. It is not general enough to become a main-sequence move, and it is too operationally relevant to be exiled to an opening aside or a vague after-note."*

**Skeptic's first-class minority** [01c, Q3]: *"C4 should not produce artefact body
text…a single sentence in the opening framing or after-note acknowledging that
domain-specific intricacies exist, without enumerating them."* The Skeptic
proposes: *"Some decisions carry extra bits that vary by kind — clearing out
what's being replaced, coordinating with a tradesperson, timing around seasons.
Handle those where they fit; the five moves above are the shape of the
conversation, not the whole job."*

The Skeptic's position has a substantive argument behind it: the user's own framing
("a few intricacies which vary by domain") diagnoses this as a scope-boundary
observation rather than a content request [01c, Q6]: *"The user isn't asking for
the artefact to cover disposal; the user is asking for the artefact to acknowledge
that stuff like disposal exists outside its frame."*

**Synthesis Q3 position:** Adopt the 3-of-4 cross-model placement (conditional
note within the close move), locating it in the restructured Move 5 per the
Reviser+Outsider Move 5 consensus (consistent with the Q2 restructured-in-place
decision).

The Skeptic's minority is preserved as first-class dissent with its own
falsifiability condition: if a future domain-actor reports (should future domain
validation become available) that the conditional note reads as domain-specific
scaffold rather than useful prompt — e.g., that the couple uses the artefact for
a non-replacement decision and feels the note is distracting or implies
enumeration of other domains — the Skeptic's single-sentence-acknowledgment
alternative is the explicit warrant for moving the conditional out of Move 5
into a framing mention.

**Candidate disposal-note text** (drawing Reviser's four-option list in user's
own order, Householder's "part of the follow-through too" structural framing,
Outsider's "offer the old shelf first" specific example):

```markdown
If something is being replaced, the old one is part of the follow-through too. Reuse it somewhere else, offer it to someone, donate it, or arrange disposal. This is often more work than people expect.

> *"We'll offer the old shelf first, then donate it if no one takes it."*
```

Placement: inside Move 5, after the "Not a full plan" paragraph but before the
"if you're not ready to close" paragraph. Sequence: decision-closed → follow-through-
named → if-replacing-also-name-what-happens-to-old → not-ready-to-close-alternative.

**Outsider's Claude-flattening flag** [01d, Q1 & Q6]: The question form *carries*
social permission (attention, timing, the right to say "not right now") that
Claude-family models can flatten into neat naming devices. This is a novel
cross-model observation that shapes the Q1 example selection.

**User-unavailability-for-future-domain-validation — four-way position tally:**

- [Reviser, Q6]: *mild* — names artefact-sprawl risk; notes that lost validation
  pressures the methodology toward more artefact, when the correct response is
  less.
- [Householder, Q6]: *cautious* — the methodology should not refuse external-
  artefact production but should not treat it as routine; n=2 is a narrow
  evidence base.
- [Skeptic, Q6]: *strong* — Session 014+ should not produce new external
  artefacts until the validation-source question is resolved; pause by default,
  unpause only via explicit argument.
- [Outsider, Q6]: *policy-choice-required* — future external artefacts no longer
