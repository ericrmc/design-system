## Q1. Move 1 revision (corrections C1 and C2)

The current Move 1 fails at the level of its first verb. *"Say what you're deciding — one sentence is enough"* is imperative, declarative, and treats the decision as already formed. The user's correction is precise: the conversation begins as a question. I take this as a register fix, not a structural one. Move 1's design purpose — giving the conversation a name so both people know what they're talking about — remains; what changes is that the name can be a question, and the constraints the original move packed into its example line (scope, budget, timing) get pushed out of Move 1 entirely, to surface where they actually surface, which is mid-conversation.

Proposed replacement text, quoted in full:

> ## 1. Name the decision
>
> Open it as a question, not a verdict. "Should we talk about the lounge?" or "What should we do about that corner of the garden?" — enough to let the other person step in.
>
> You don't need to know the budget, the timing, or the shape of the answer yet. Those usually show up later, once you've both said what you care about and looked at what's actually available. Naming the thing is enough.
>
> If you're not sure it's even worth a real conversation, say that too. Sometimes the answer is "let's just do it." Sometimes naming the decision out loud is the only move you need.

Word-level choices I want to defend. *Open it as a question, not a verdict* — "verdict" is harder than "declarative sentence" and carries the correct charge: the problem with the original Move 1 wasn't that it used a full stop, it was that it pre-judged the answer. *Step in* is deliberately softer than "join the conversation" or "respond"; the user's own examples ("Should we talk about...", "What should we do about...") are invitations, and the partner's move is acceptance of an invitation, not participation in a proceeding. *Those usually show up later, once you've both said what you care about and looked at what's actually available* — I'm naming Moves 2 and 3 by their functions without naming them by number, which keeps Move 1 individually addressable (design constraint) while honouring the user's observation that constraints surface during the conversation.

The two example openings I propose are both user-sourced (I paraphrased the garden one, but it's in the same grammatical mood as the user's lounge example). I did not invent a third to round out the list; two is enough to show a pattern, and a third would start to feel like a menu.

The final paragraph is preserved verbatim. The brief explicitly invites preservation unless I have a specific reason to revise, and I don't.

Alternative framing I considered and rejected: a more minimal edit that kept the imperative frame but softened it — something like *"Start with a question — one sentence is enough. 'Should we talk about the lounge?'"*. This would have been a lighter touch on the original and would have retained Move 1's existing silhouette. I rejected it for two reasons. First, it still front-loads "one sentence is enough," which, combined with the existing "Say what you're deciding," sets an expectation of compression the user has told us is wrong for this move — the opening is often *less* than a sentence, it's a question whose answer is not yet the decision itself. Second, it does nothing with C2: it would leave the reader still looking for an example that demonstrates the register, and the flagged corporate example would need to be replaced anyway, so the light-touch edit doesn't save words net. The fuller rewrite above spends three paragraphs where the original spent three, trades the corporate example for two casual question-form ones, and adds the surfaces-later paragraph as direct scaffold for Move 2 and Move 3's jobs.

One thing I want to flag. The original's *"one sentence is enough"* framing is load-bearing for a certain kind of reader — the one who over-prepares and writes a paragraph when a line would do. My replacement loses that reader a little, because the replacement says "a question is enough," which is a different kind of permission. I think this is the right trade given C1 and C2, but a synthesis that wants to keep the original's compression-permission could add back a half-line — something like *"a short one"* after "a question." I'd advise against it: the second paragraph already delivers the compression-permission by relocating all the known-up-front pressure to "show up later."

## Q2. Move 5 expansion (correction C3)

I propose **restructured Move 5** — a rewrite that handles both closing and naming the owner within one move, with the move's title adjusted to reflect both functions. Not a new Move 6; not an inline tacked-on line.

Here is my argument, and then the text.

The user describes the follow-up burden as continuous with the close: *"to close it, we also need to name the person responsible for following up."* The "also" is the telling word. The couple, in my reading of the user's feedback, will experience closing the decision and naming the owner as a single completed action — the moment the decision is real. Separating them into Move 5 and Move 6 would imply that a decision can be properly closed without naming who does the work, which is precisely the gap the user is flagging. A new Move 6 would also add a numbered step for what is really one beat with two parts, and Move 5's current structure (someone says it out loud; the other confirms) accommodates the second part without losing rhythm.

On awkward hierarchy: the risk is that "naming the person responsible" sounds like one partner assigning a task to the other. Language here matters more than structure. If the move's text assumes the owner is named by the person who will do the work, or by mutual agreement in the same breath, the hierarchy dissolves. If the text reads like an assignment from a project manager, it doesn't. My wording below leans on *"one of you takes it on"* and *"whoever's following up"* — the owner emerges from the conversation rather than being appointed.

On the mental burden the user named (schedule, order, delivery-or-pickup, availability): I think naming these in Move 5 risks turning a household move into a project-management checklist, which the artefact's register refuses. But the move has to acknowledge that following up is real work, not a formality — otherwise the "also name the owner" addition reads as decorative. I handle this by naming the *shape* of the work ("what it'll take to actually make it happen") without enumerating the specific tasks, and by letting the owner name their own next step rather than having the artefact prescribe it. C4's disposal-sub-sequence, which I address in Q3, handles one of the domain-specific parts of the burden separately.

Proposed replacement text for Move 5, quoted in full:

> ## 5. Close it, and name who's following up
>
> Someone says it out loud:
>
> > *Okay — bookshelves, up to $500, ordering this weekend. I'll handle it.*
>
> The other confirms. Whoever's following up names what it'll take to actually make it happen — ordering, scheduling, being home for delivery, whatever the thing needs. Not a full plan, just enough that you both know what they've taken on.
>
> If you're not ready to close, say that too, and name when you'll come back to it — a specific day, not "soon."

Register notes. *Okay — bookshelves, up to $500, ordering this weekend. I'll handle it.* — I've collapsed the original's formal *"We've decided — we're going with the bookshelves"* into something a partner would actually say at a kitchen table. "We've decided" is the verbal tic of minutes being taken; "Okay" is how two people close a conversation in their own house. The *"I'll handle it"* is the C3 correction living inside the example line rather than appearing as a separate instruction.

*Not a full plan, just enough that you both know what they've taken on* — this line is doing the heavy lifting against scope creep. It explicitly forbids the artefact from turning into a task list while still acknowledging that the burden exists.

Alternatives I considered and rejected. An inline addition to current Move 5 — something like appending *"Name who's following up."* as a fourth paragraph — would have been the minimal edit. I rejected it because the original's example line (*"We've decided — we're going with the bookshelves, up to $500, ordering this weekend"*) is itself part of what the user is implicitly correcting. The original example names scope, budget, and timing but not the owner, which is exactly the gap C3 flags. An inline addition would leave the example line intact, teaching the reader by example that the owner is an afterthought. The example line has to change too; once it changes, the whole move is close enough to a rewrite that a single restructured move reads cleaner than a minimal edit with a bolt-on.

A separate Move 6 for the owner — I rejected this above on the continuous-action argument, but I also reject it on the shape-of-the-page argument. Moves 1 through 4 each do something that would still be missing if skipped; a Move 6 that only says "name who's following up" does not earn its own header alongside "Name the decision," "Say what each of you cares about," "Narrow to real options," "Look for the workable yes," and "Close it." It reads as inflation.

## Q3. Handling replaced items (correction C4)

> If the new thing is replacing something — furniture, an appliance, anything you've had a while — add the old thing to what's being taken on. Reuse it somewhere else, offer it to someone, donate it, or work out how to get rid of it. That's part of closing out, not a separate problem.
