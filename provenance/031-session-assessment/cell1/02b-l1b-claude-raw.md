# A Lying-Down Exploration of Your Own Posture

## Setting up

Find a firm flat floor. Carpet is fine. A thin blanket or yoga mat underneath you is fine; a thick mattress is not — the floor is part of the instrument. You want something that gives back when you press into it.

You'll be here for about 25 to 30 minutes. Close the door. Turn off anything that pings.

Lie on your back. Legs long, arms resting somewhere near your sides but not pinned against them. If your head tips back uncomfortably, slide a folded towel — no more than an inch or two thick — under your skull, not your neck. That's the only prop you might want.

Take a minute here before doing anything else.

---

## 1. The first inventory

Without moving, begin to notice where your body meets the floor.

Start at your heels. Do both heels touch the floor with the same weight, or does one press down more than the other? Don't adjust. Just notice.

Move your attention up. The backs of your calves — do they touch the floor at all? One more than the other? What about the backs of your knees: is there a gap, and if so, how big?

Your pelvis. Which parts of the back of your pelvis are on the floor? Is the contact even across the left and right?

Your lower back. How much space is there between your lumbar curve and the floor? Could you slide a hand under? A flat palm? A whole forearm?

Your upper back, your shoulder blades. Does one shoulder blade feel more settled than the other? Does one shoulder ride higher — closer to your ear?

The back of your head. Is it centered, or does it feel like it's fallen slightly to one side?

You're not fixing anything. You're taking a map.

*What you find here will not match what someone else finds. There is no correct map.*

Rest for a few breaths.

---

## 2. The legs

Slowly slide your right heel along the floor toward your buttock, bending the knee. Go only as far as is easy. Then slide it back out. Do this three or four times, slowly.

Notice: which muscles do you feel working? Does the foot track straight, or does the knee drift inward or outward as you slide? Does the hip on that side lift off the floor, or stay put?

Rest with both legs long. Does the right leg now feel different from the left? Longer? Heavier? More connected to the floor? Or does nothing feel different — that's also an honest answer.

Now do the same with the left leg. Three or four slow slides. Pay attention to the same things. Notice whether it feels like the same movement on this side, or a different one.

Rest. Both legs long again. Take stock.

---

## 3. The arms and shoulders

Bring your attention to your right arm. Very slowly, slide it along the floor out to the side — away from your body, palm up — until it reaches roughly shoulder height. Then slide it back.

Did the arm stay on the floor the whole way, or did parts of it lift? Where did it lift? Did your right shoulder blade move on the floor, or did it stay stuck?

Do this two more times, slowly, attending to the path the arm takes and what the shoulder blade does.

Now the left arm. The same slow slide out and back, two or three times.

Was one side heavier? Smoother? Did one shoulder blade glide while the other dragged?

Rest. Arms back where they started. Feel the whole back of your torso against the floor. Has anything changed since your first inventory?

---

## 4. The head

Very gently, let your head roll a small amount to the right. Not a big turn — just let it tip. Then let it come back to center. Then to the left. Then back.

Do this slowly, maybe four or five times each direction. Notice whether the head rolls the same amount to each side, or whether one side feels shorter, stickier, or like it hits something sooner.

Stop in the middle. Without turning your head, imagine a line from the crown of your head down through your body. Does your head feel centered on that line, or slightly off to one side?

Rest.

---

## 5. The whole length

Bring your attention back to the floor underneath you. Slowly, move your attention from heels to head again, the way you did at the beginning.

The heels. The calves. The backs of the knees. The pelvis. The lower back — how much space now? The same as before? More? Less? Different on one side than the other?

The upper back. The shoulder blades. The shoulders. The back of the head.

*Different readers will find different things here. Some people feel their whole back has softened into the floor. Some feel one side has changed while the other hasn't. Some feel almost nothing has shifted. All of these are information about what your body was doing before you started.*

You are not trying to reach a conclusion. You are building a more detailed picture than you had half an hour ago.

---

## 6. Closing

Stay lying down for another minute or two. Let the map settle.

When you're ready to get up, don't sit straight up. Roll onto one side — whichever side you prefer. Pause there. Then push yourself up with your arms to sitting, letting your head come up last.

Sit for a moment on the floor. Then stand, slowly.

As you stand, don't try to apply anything. Just notice, for a few seconds, whether anything feels different from how you usually feel standing. It may. It may not. Either is a real answer.

That's the end. You have a slightly more detailed sense of yourself than you did before. That is the whole point.
