Let yourself arrive on the floor. Take the next few moments to do nothing about yourself. No fixing, no stretching, no arranging for an ideal position. Just let the floor hold you exactly as you are.

Place your legs wherever they can rest easily. Let your arms be somewhere comfortable, a little away from your sides if that works, or closer in if that is what you are doing already. Allow your eyes to close, or leave them softly open without looking around.

Begin by noticing the places where you meet the floor. Do not search everywhere at once. Pick one simple area. The back of your head. Your shoulder blades. Your ribs. Your pelvis. Your heels. One place at a time. Notice where the contact feels clear, where it feels faint, and where there seems to be no contact at all.

Now let your attention move slowly from your head toward your feet. Move at a pace that lets you actually feel, rather than name. Your jaw. Your tongue. Your throat. The back of your neck. Across your shoulders. Down both arms to your hands. Across your chest and upper back. Around your waist. Through your pelvis. Down each leg. Into your feet. If you lose track, pause and begin again from wherever you are.

Notice your breathing, but do not improve it. Let the breath be ordinary. Feel what moves when air comes in, and what settles when air goes out. Notice whether the movement is more visible in your chest, your belly, your sides, your back, or somewhere else. Notice whether one side participates more than the other. Stay with that for two or three breaths.

Now become a little more specific. Compare the right side of yourself with the left side. Does one shoulder feel heavier? Does one leg turn out more? Does one side of your waist feel longer or shorter against the floor? Do not correct anything. Just register what you find.

Bring attention to your head and face. Is your head turned a little to one side? Are your eyes resting evenly in their sockets, or does one side of your face feel more active? Is your mouth soft, or are your lips, tongue, or teeth doing extra work? Let these details appear without changing them.

Shift your attention to your ribs and spine. Notice whether the center of your back feels broad on the floor or held away from it. Notice if one side of your rib cage feels more open. Feel the small space under your low back. You do not need to flatten it or increase it. Just notice that it is there, and notice whether it changes as you breathe.

Now to your pelvis and legs. Feel the weight of your pelvis. Is it centered, or does more weight seem to drop into one side? Notice the direction of your knees and feet without moving them. Do both legs rest in the same way? Is one foot heavier, cooler, more outward, more alive?

For the last few moments, sense your whole shape on the floor at once. Your outline. Your weight. Your breathing. Your asymmetries. Your familiar places of effort and your familiar places of absence. Let yourself quietly recognize: this is how you are arriving today.

Take one easy breath in, and a longer breath out. Stay where you are.
