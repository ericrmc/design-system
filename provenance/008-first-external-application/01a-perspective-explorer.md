## Q1

Three candidates, chosen to span genuinely different vocabularies and problem-shapes. I have deliberately avoided any domain whose native artefact is a specification or a document-like deliverable, because criterion 4 is the one I most distrust being gamed.

**Candidate A — Movement / Choreography: a short solo sequence for a named constraint.**
- Domain: movement design (choreography, exercise sequencing, or physical-practice routines — e.g. a rehabilitation sequence, a warm-up routine, a short dance phrase).
- Problem: design a ~60-second solo movement sequence for one stated constraint the user names (e.g. "morning mobility for a stiff lower back," "a thirty-second transition between two named postures," "a practice-room phrase built around one refused motion").
- Example problem-shapes: (i) a graded mobility sequence the user could actually try over a week; (ii) a movement exercise that explores a single "forbidden" transition.
- Strongest criteria: 4 (vocabulary is genuinely foreign — tempo, weight, impulse, phrasing, breath — none of which map to specs), 3 (Validate is the user attempting the sequence and reporting felt-sense), 2 (low stakes if advisory and non-injurious).
- Weakest fit: 5 (external legibility — a reader without movement training may not know whether it is "real choreography" or a list of steps wearing movement vocabulary). Mitigated if the artefact is a notated score plus a rationale in movement-terms, not a timeline.

**Candidate B — Recipe design: a dish built around a constraint the user names.**
- Domain: culinary design.
- Problem: design a dish (not a menu, not a cuisine) built around one stated constraint — a pantry inventory, a dietary restriction the user names, a cooking-method limit (e.g. "only one pan"), or a flavour intention ("a warming dish with no dairy and no added sugar").
- Example problem-shapes: (i) a weeknight dish for a named ingredient the user has too much of; (ii) a dish that substitutes for a named dish the user cannot currently eat.
- Strongest criteria: 7 (fully accessible — the user can cook and taste it), 3 (Validate = cook and eat, with reported outcome), 1 (bounded — one dish, one session).
- Weakest fit: 4 (recipes are document-shaped; a recipe artefact may end up looking too much like a spec). Has to be pushed by making the deliberation-work visible — substitutions considered, flavour-balance reasoning, technique choice with alternatives — not just the final recipe card.

**Candidate C — Garden / planting bed design: a small plot under stated conditions.**
- Domain: horticultural / planting design.
- Problem: design a planting plan for a small bed (balcony, window box, one raised bed, a named corner of a garden) under the user's stated site conditions (aspect, sun hours, soil or container, climate zone).
- Example problem-shapes: (i) a three-season herb-and-edible bed for a stated balcony; (ii) a pollinator-friendly corner under partial shade.
- Strongest criteria: 4 (vocabulary: succession, companion planting, bloom-time, root-depth, microclimate — foreign), 5 (a planting plan reads as "real garden design" to anyone who has planted anything), 1 (bounded).
- Weakest fit: 3 (Validate is seasonal — real outcomes are months away; mitigated by making the first-session Validate a peer-plausibility check against stated rules, with the real Validate deferred as multi-session follow-up).

Rejected adjacent alternatives: three variants of "curriculum" (explicitly barred by the brief); three flavours of writing (essay, poem, short story) — too document-shaped to stress criterion 4; "design a meditation script" — too close to methodology's own talk-about-practice register.

## Q2

I recommend **Candidate A — the movement sequence.**

It handles criterion 4 most seriously. The nine activities will be forced to re-interpret, not translate. "Read" becomes reading the constraint and the body's available vocabulary; "Assess" becomes inventorying what the body can already do and what it cannot; "Deliberate" becomes weighing alternative transitions, impulses, phrasings; "Produce" becomes notating or describing a sequence that must be executable by a body, not by an agent; "Validate" becomes the user attempting the sequence and reporting felt outcomes. None of this reduces to writing a spec. If the methodology can produce something a body can do, the vocabulary has genuinely travelled.

Criteria handled best: 4 (vocabulary distance — this is the one), 3 (Validate is unambiguous and fast — the user tries it), 2 (advisory, non-injurious if scoped to gentle mobility), 1 (one sequence, one session).

The property I, as selecting agent, expect to be challenging: **the artefact-format problem.** Movement is primarily non-textual. Any artefact produced in this workspace will be textual — a score, a step-list, a rationale in prose. There is a real risk that "movement design rendered as text" is still too document-shaped, and that the methodology ends up producing a choreography notation that is a specification by another name. That would be a criterion-4 failure disguised as success.

Why proceed anyway: because the Validate analogue rescues it. The user can try the sequence. If the sequence does not work as movement — if transitions are impossible, if tempo is unsustainable, if the "forbidden motion" constraint is not actually honoured — that is legible failure in movement-terms, not spec-terms. The test-quality is in the execution loop, not the notation. Textual notation is not the test; embodied execution is. This meets my stance: distant vocabulary *and* accessible *and* a legible Validate analogue.

If the adversarial reader is correct that this fails too hard — if the user cannot ratify a movement target because it feels too unfamiliar to judge — I would demote A to shortlist and promote **C (planting)** instead. C is vocabulary-distant and externally legible, but its Validate analogue is weak in one session. I hold A more strongly because Validate is first-session-real.

I would not recommend B (recipe) as first pick, despite its accessibility. It is the candidate closest to document-shaped output, and it is the one most likely to produce a result that reads like "the methodology did a recipe" rather than "a real recipe the methodology helped design." It is the safe pick, which by my stance is the weakest evidence.

## Q3

Two target-specific failure modes for Candidate A.

**Failure mode 1 — Notation-as-specification.** The artefact is a written movement score. The risk is that the methodology's output describes the sequence with the same granularity and vocabulary shape it uses for its own specs — numbered steps, each with a rationale block, each cross-referenced, each audited. A dance score does not read like that. A real movement artefact has timing (counts, breaths), weight-and-impulse annotations, floor-pattern or orientation notes, and typically assumes the reader's body as a co-author. If the methodology produces a 14-step numbered list with "Rationale: transitions honour the stated constraint," it has failed at criterion 4 regardless of how well-reasoned the content is.

Evidence that distinguishes this from a methodology failure: **show the artefact to a movement-literate reader (the user suffices if they have any movement background, or a friend who dances / does yoga / does physio)** and ask "does this read like a phrase someone could learn, or like a document about a phrase?" If the answer is "document about," the target exposed a genuine translation gap. This is a target property (movement resists text) not a methodology property (methodology over-specifies). The target is the one that revealed the over-specifying tendency the methodology already had.

**Failure mode 2 — Constraint under-specification by the user.** The user provides a constraint that is too loose ("design a warm-up") or too tight ("design a sequence in 4/4 at 120bpm with the following eight poses") and the methodology either (a) designs generic movement that does not honour any real constraint, or (b) fills in the constraint by picking tempo/poses itself, which means the methodology designed the problem and the solution and has no real external pressure. Both produce artefacts that look fine but prove nothing.

Evidence: the Deliberate record should show alternatives considered *within* the constraint space. If alternatives are all generic ("faster vs. slower," "more vs. fewer poses") rather than constraint-native ("prioritise breath over tempo," "refuse the named motion by routing through X instead of Y"), the constraint was inert. This is a target failure (constraint was not load-bearing) not a methodology failure (Deliberate did its job with the constraint it had).

A shared diagnostic for both: run the artefact past someone who does the domain. If they say "that's not how we talk about this" or "that's not a phrase, that's a list" — target-genuine failure, which is good for learning. If they say "the reasoning is clear but over-formal" — methodology-shape failure, which is different and worth its own fix.

To Session 008's Validate: these two failure-hypotheses should be probe-items. Did the user (or a friend) try the sequence, and did it read as a phrase? Did the constraint pressure the Deliberate alternatives, or were the alternatives generic?

## Q4

An external reader — someone movement-literate who has never read a line of this methodology — should see the first external artefact and judge it as movement design by movement criteria, not methodology criteria.

Concretely, the artefact should contain:

- **A performable score.** Not a numbered step-list. A sequence with counts or breath-markers, directional notes, weight shifts, and transitions named in movement terms (e.g. "from seated, spiral through left side, arriving on hands and knees; four breaths"). The score should be *trainable* — another body could attempt it.
- **A constraint-honouring rationale, in movement vocabulary.** If the constraint was "design a thirty-second transition between two named postures that refuses a specific motion," the rationale should name *which motion was refused*, *what was routed through instead*, and *why the chosen route honours the refusal better than the alternative routes considered*. Not "we selected this because it satisfied the criteria." Rather: "a direct hip hinge would have re-introduced the refused motion; the chosen spiral preserves the orientation without it."
- **At least one rejected alternative, in movement vocabulary.** "Considered a lateral rather than a rotational transition; rejected because lateral motion in this tempo compresses the breath."

The reader's judgement test: would this be a legible short phrase in their discipline? A yoga teacher would recognise it as a short sequence, whether they would teach it exactly this way or not. A physio would recognise a mobility sequence. A dancer would recognise a study or a short phrase. If none of them recognise it as an instance of their domain's vocabulary — if it reads only as "a document describing a movement sequence" — criterion 5 is failed.

What makes it "real design package for movement" rather than "methodology's processes narrated in movement vocabulary":

1. **The artefact would survive extraction.** If someone copied only the score and rationale out of the provenance file and pinned it to a studio wall, it would still function as a movement instruction. If extracted, it collapses into "methodology notes about movement," then the methodology is load-bearing in a way a real design package should not require.
2. **The rationale uses movement's own terms, not the methodology's.** No "triggers," no "manifests," no "deliberation schema." The movement-native terms (impulse, phrasing, weight, orientation) do the work.
3. **The rejected alternatives are movement-rejections, not procedural rejections.** "Rejected because the body cannot sustain this tempo in this breath pattern" is domain-native. "Rejected because it failed criterion X" is methodology-native.

What criterion 5 most clearly filters out: the temptation to produce a long provenance file with a small movement artefact embedded. A reader should be able to find the artefact inside ninety seconds. If the methodology's process is louder than the movement, the external reader has no way to judge the domain work.

## Q5

The bias I want to name: **the selecting agent over-weights domains whose work-products are textual and linear.** Criterion 8 guards against software-adjacency. Criterion 6 guards against expected-to-succeed. Neither directly guards the assumption that the methodology's artefact-format (documents, with numbered structure and prose rationale) can carry the work in any domain. This is a deeper bias than "software-adjacent" — it is "document-adjacent." Curricula, recipes, essays, learning plans, style guides, policy briefs, editorial style-sheets — all feel "natural" to methodology work because their native artefact is already a document. The methodology does not have to re-interpret how outputs exist; it only has to swap vocabulary.

This is why I treated recipes (Candidate B) with suspicion and pushed movement (Candidate A) as primary. Not because text-artefact domains are bad, but because the agent's pull toward them is strong enough that they produce weak evidence. A win in a text-artefact domain is ambiguous: did the methodology's reasoning travel, or did its formatting travel?

**A candidate I would rule out on this scrutiny: any "design a reading list / curriculum / learning path for topic X."** The brief itself warns against three-curricula shortlists, but I extend the warning: a single curriculum candidate is already over-weighted by this bias. The methodology will feel at home — sequenced units, rationale for ordering, rejected alternatives, a Validate that is "does the learner report learning." It will produce a curriculum that looks fine. It will prove almost nothing.

**A candidate I would add on this scrutiny, and offer for consideration though not on my Q1 shortlist: a small physical-arrangement design** — a desk layout, a room re-arrangement for a stated constraint, a route or walk planned for stated conditions. Artefacts are diagrammatic or spatial, not linear-textual. Vocabulary (sight-line, reach, flow, affordance) is genuinely different. Validate is the user living with it for a few days. Weaker on external legibility in this workspace because diagrams are hard to produce here, which is why I did not advance it — but on vocabulary-distance it is probably stronger than any of my three.

The meta-point: the non-obvious bias is not about *which domain* but about *which artefact-shape*. If every candidate produces a document, the first external test is not very external.

## Meta-note

My position most likely to differ from the other perspectives: I will push harder than the others for movement (or any non-document-shaped domain), and I expect the Pragmatist to prefer recipe or planting, the Skeptic to prefer a writing/editing task, and the Outsider to possibly suggest something more unusual but still text-native. My Q5 claim — that artefact-shape bias is more load-bearing than domain bias — is where I expect to be most alone, and I hold it deliberately at its strongest.

Assumption in the brief I flag as suspect: the claim that "mid vocabulary distance" is defined by "differs from methodology's native self-description but overlaps enough to translate." Translation-overlap is what makes a candidate feel tractable, but translation is exactly the mechanism by which a test-result can look like success without being one. Criterion 4 as written may be too lenient.

Concern about framing: the brief reads criterion 7 (accessibility) and criterion 6 (not-pre-shaped) as counter-weights. In practice, 7 will do more work than 6, because accessibility is concrete and pre-shaping is diagnosed post-hoc. This asymmetry favours safe picks.
