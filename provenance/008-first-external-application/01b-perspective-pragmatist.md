## Q1

Three candidates that clear D-046, drawn from domains where the user can plausibly supply source material and the output is a judgeable artefact in one session.

**Candidate A — Personal reading list curation with an argued spine.** Domain: reading-list design for a self-directed learner. Problem: given a topic the user is actively reading into (candidate shapes: "Bayesian reasoning for a generalist," "the history of mechanical watches," "how insurance pricing actually works"), produce a 10–15 item reading list with reading order, a one-paragraph rationale per item, a declared spine (the argument the list is trying to teach), and two deliberately rejected-but-named alternatives per slot. The artefact is a single document. Validate analogue: a rival-list check — the methodology-produced list is scored against a public canonical list (textbook bibliography, expert syllabus) the user can find in under an hour, on coverage, ordering defensibility, and whether the spine is articulable. Satisfies 1, 3, 5, 7 cleanly. Weakest: 4 (vocabulary distance is thin — "list of things with reasons" is close to "specification with provenance"), and 6 (too comfortable).

**Candidate B — A training-plan design for a physical skill with measurable progressions.** Domain: training-plan design. Problem: produce an 8–12 week plan for a specific physical goal the user names (shapes: "first unassisted pull-up," "run 10km continuously from a 5km baseline," "hold a 30-second freestanding handstand"). Artefact: weekly blocks, session structure, progression rules, deload logic, failure-branch decisions ("if week 4 fails the progression test, do X"). Validate analogue: compare progression rules against two published programs in the sub-discipline on load, deload cadence, and adaptation model; additionally the user can execute session 1 in the real world and report whether the plan was coachable. Satisfies 1, 2, 3, 5, 7 strongly. Weakest: 6 is the concern — this is moderately comfortable, though less than A. Also partially 2 — low-probability injury risk exists but mitigated by user-as-adult-athlete.

**Candidate C — A household decision-design package for a bounded one-off purchase.** Domain: consumer-decision design. Problem: produce a decision package for a concrete purchase the user is actually contemplating (shapes: "next laptop," "a specific appliance replacement," "choosing a bike"). Artefact: criteria set with weights and argued origins, three to five candidates evaluated, sensitivity notes, explicit what-would-change-the-decision triggers, a recommended pick. Validate analogue: a reviewer pass by someone the user knows who bought in the same category recently, scoring whether the criteria match how real buyers decide; plus a six-month revisit trigger. Satisfies 1, 2, 3, 5, 7. Weakest: 4 (decision-matrices read close to methodology's native decision-record vocabulary), and 8 adjacency if the purchase is software-ish.

Rejected: curriculum design for a public audience (unbounded, no real reviewer); recipe-development (sensory Validate requires fieldwork); meditation-practice design (too subjective for 5).

## Q2

I recommend **Candidate B — training-plan design**.

B handles the hardest criteria best. Criterion 3 (Validate analogue): a training plan is judgeable against published programs on axes that are technical rather than taste-based (load progression, volume/intensity ratios, deload cadence, adaptation model named). Criterion 5 (external legibility): any reader who has trained for a physical goal recognises what a plan looks like — reps, sets, progression rules, deload weeks — without needing a single methodology term. Criterion 7 (accessibility): the user can state the goal and their current baseline in three sentences; no proprietary data needed. Criterion 1 (bounded): one session produces the plan; execution is multi-session for the user, but the artefact is complete. Criterion 2: low-stakes advisory; the user is an adult operator who will apply judgement.

The property I expect will be challenging — and this is my criterion-6 declaration — is **Validate strain under adaptation uncertainty**. A training plan's correctness is partly verifiable in the room (does this follow known progression principles?) and partly only verifiable by running it (does the user actually adapt?). The methodology cannot run the plan in one session. So Validate will have to rest on rule-comparison against published programs plus a session-1 coachability check, not on outcome evidence. That is a thinner Validate than I would want, and it's exactly where a methodology-shaped artefact could fake depth by adding provenance around rules that were borrowed wholesale.

I proceed nonetheless because: (a) the rule-comparison is concrete enough to expose fakery — if the methodology's plan cannot justify why it chose linear vs. undulating periodisation, a reader notices immediately; (b) the one-session coachability check is a real-world signal the user can execute in under an hour; and (c) the alternative — demanding outcome-verified Validate — rules out nearly every interesting first target and reduces the methodology to post-hoc narration of completed work.

Adversarial to myself: if the user's specific goal is one where published programs are scarce or contested (e.g., a niche skill), Validate collapses and I would demote B to the shortlist and recommend C instead. C's Validate is weaker but more executable — a single recent-buyer reviewer is a firmer signal than comparison against contested literature.

I'm holding B over C because B's vocabulary distance (criterion 4) is clearly better: "progression rules, deload, adaptation stimulus" is a genuinely different language from "decisions, deliberations, specifications" — C's decision-matrix language is adjacent to the methodology's decision-record language in ways that invite confirmation-bias concern under criterion 8 even if not formally software.

## Q3

Two target-specific failure modes on the training-plan pick.

**Failure mode 1 — Generic-plan dressing.** The methodology produces a plan that could be lifted from any textbook with the user's name substituted in, with provenance annotations explaining why "3 sets of 5" was chosen when the real reason is "every beginner program says so." The failure here is that the target admits a vast corpus of well-established programs; the methodology's "deliberation" becomes ceremonial because the right answer is substantially pre-decided by the literature. Distinguishing evidence: if the Produce artefact closely matches (say) Starting Strength or Couch-to-5k in structure, and the provenance does not name the rejected alternative (undulating periodisation, high-frequency low-volume, etc.) with a domain-specific reason for rejection, the methodology has narrated rather than designed. The test: can the artefact articulate *why not* a defensible alternative, in the alternative's own vocabulary? If every rejection reduces to "the chosen option is more canonical," that is methodology-talk, not domain work.

**Failure mode 2 — Individualisation theatre.** The plan appears personalised (references the user's baseline, their training history, their schedule constraints) but the personalisation is cosmetic — the progression rules, volume, and recovery logic do not actually change based on the stated individual facts. The failure here is that the target rewards apparent customisation; a reader skimming sees "tailored to Eric's 5km baseline and Tuesday/Thursday availability" and gives credit, but an expert reader sees that the same plan would have been produced for any 5km baseline with any two-day schedule. Distinguishing evidence: if the Validate activity asks "name two user-specific facts that meaningfully changed a choice and show the counterfactual plan without those facts," and the answer is vague or the counterfactual is identical, personalisation was performed, not done. This is target-specific because training-plan design culturally rewards visible personalisation regardless of whether it is load-bearing.

Both failures are **target** failures in the sense that the target's domain invites them — training-plan design is a field where canonical answers exist and where cosmetic tailoring is common. They would not indict the methodology; they would indicate that this target over-rewards surface moves the methodology does well.

Evidence distinguishing target-failure from methodology-failure: if the user ratifies the plan, executes session 1, reports it was coachable, and a peer who trains in the same sub-discipline judges the rejection-rationale as domain-native — the methodology worked but on a target that made the work easy to fake. If the user cannot execute session 1 because the plan's instructions are internally inconsistent or ambiguous, that is a methodology failure (the Produce activity did not yield a usable artefact regardless of target). If the provenance record cannot articulate any choice in domain terms, that is a methodology failure.

## Q4

A reader who has never read this methodology picks up the artefact. What they should see: a document titled something like "8-Week Plan: First Unassisted Pull-Up from a Dead Hang of 0" or "10km Continuous from a 5km Baseline in 10 Weeks." The opening half-page states the goal, the baseline assumed, the schedule, and the adaptation model the plan rests on (e.g., progressive overload via assisted-volume tapering, or aerobic base-building via heart-rate capped zone-2 plus weekly tempo). Then week blocks — each week has session-by-session prescriptions: exercise, sets, reps or duration, intensity cue (RPE, HR zone, %1RM if the sport uses it), and a brief note on what that session is targeting. Progression rules are explicit: "if week 3 session 2 achieves X, advance to Y in week 4; else repeat week 3." Deload logic is named. Failure branches exist.

What makes it read as real design for this domain: (a) **domain vocabulary without methodology leakage** — the word "specification" does not appear; "deliberation" does not appear; provenance is framed as "why this choice" in coach-speak, not decision-record language; (b) **trade-offs named in the sub-discipline's own terms** — for a pull-up plan, discussion of scapular strength vs. elbow flexion vs. grip endurance, not a generic "we weighed options"; (c) **commitments that would embarrass a lazy plan** — e.g., "by week 5, the user should hold a 15-second dead hang; if not, return to week 3 and add grip-specific volume" (specific, falsifiable, coachable); (d) **rejected-alternatives in domain language** — "we did not choose high-frequency daily pulling because the user's two-day schedule and current connective-tissue readiness do not support the recovery cost," not "option B was rejected after deliberation."

What makes it read as methodology-in-costume: references to "the deliberation" or "the decision-record"; criteria with numerical weights summed to justify the plan; provenance written in the workspace's internal vocabulary; rejection reasons that are procedural ("alternative X did not satisfy criterion 3") rather than domain-substantive.

The reader's judgement heuristic: could they hand this plan to a friend and say "follow this for eight weeks"? If yes, the artefact is real work. If their reaction is "interesting framework but I don't know what to do on Tuesday," the artefact has narrated a process rather than produced a plan. Criterion 5 is satisfied when the plan is executable by someone who does not know the methodology exists.

A secondary test: show the artefact to a coach or experienced trainee the user knows. If their feedback is substantive ("the deload at week 4 is too early given the volume in weeks 1-3"), the artefact has engaged the domain. If their feedback is "I can't tell what this is," legibility failed.

## Q5

The non-obvious agent bias I'd flag: **a preference for targets that produce a single primary document as the artefact**. The methodology's self-application has produced decision-records, specifications, briefs — all document-shaped. An agent selecting external targets tends to favour domains where the natural output is also a document: syllabi, plans, specs, decision packages, reading lists. This is not software-adjacency (criterion 8) and not expected-to-succeed (criterion 6 in its narrow form); it is **medium bias**. The methodology's apparatus (provenance, deliberation records, triggers-met annotations) slots neatly onto document-shaped outputs and strains on artefact-shaped outputs that are not primarily textual.

Targets this bias makes invisible: designs whose natural output is **not a document**. Examples: a **physical-space arrangement** (how should the user arrange their desk, tools, and daily-flow objects in a home office for a named set of activities?) — the natural artefact is a diagram plus placement rationale, not a specification. A **small ritual or routine design** (a morning start-sequence, a weekly review practice) — the natural artefact is a script the user performs, and its correctness is whether the user can perform it without the script after a week. A **conversation-opening design** (how to raise a specific awkward topic with a named counterparty in the user's life) — the natural artefact is a one-page approach that is judged by the user actually having the conversation.

I will not add these to the Q1 shortlist because I'm unsure any of them survive criterion 5 (external legibility to a reader outside the user's life) or criterion 3 (Validate analogue). But they scrutinise an agent-selection prior: **if the first external application looks like another document, the methodology has chosen a target its existing apparatus already fits, which is a weaker test than a target whose output forces the apparatus to adapt**. A stronger first application might be one where the methodology has to decide what to produce, not just how to justify what it produces.

To rule out a seemingly-obvious candidate on this scrutiny: "**design a specification for the user's personal note-taking system**." This feels natural to an agent — bounded, user-accessible, vocabulary-adjacent. Reject it. It is software-adjacent in spirit (criterion 8), it is an agent-comfort domain (criterion 6), and it is pure document-output (the bias above). It would likely produce a fluent artefact and almost no external evidence.

## Meta-note

I expect The Explorer will push toward a target I would flag as over-ambitious (novel domain, weak Validate), and The Skeptic will reject one or more of my candidates as still too close to methodology comfort zones. Those are useful countervailing pressures; I record B as recommended with the criterion-6 risk named openly, not softened.

Assumption I flag as suspect: the brief frames the user as sole first reader and validator. Criterion 3's Validate analogue gets thin without a second human, and several of my candidates lean on "a knowledgeable peer" as implicit second reader. If Session 008 cannot actually reach a second reader in-session, Validate for B reduces to rule-comparison-only, which is a real weakening.

Concern about framing: the brief conflates "first external application" with "first-session-executable increment." These may be different things. The first genuinely external application might rightly take two or three sessions; the pressure to close in one session biases toward small, document-shaped, legible-fast targets — which is itself the pre-shaping criterion 6 guards against.
