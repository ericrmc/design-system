ADMISSION PROCEDURE

STAGE 1 — THE GATE PETITION

The applicant approaches the community's main entrance and requests admission of the doorkeeper, a member appointed to this duty on a rotating basis. The doorkeeper does not admit the applicant. He records the applicant's name, origin, and stated reason for coming, provides shelter at the threshold (a guest bench, an outer porch, or an unheated antechamber), and tells the applicant to remain there. The doorkeeper brings food and water at the customary times but does not engage in conversation about admission. The applicant remains at the threshold for no fewer than three days and no more than seven, during which any member who passes may speak with the applicant or ignore him as he chooses.

Advancement criterion: the applicant is still present on the morning following the third day, and at least one full member has spoken to the doorkeeper in support of further consideration. If neither condition holds, the doorkeeper informs the applicant that the community will not receive him at this time and gives him provisions for departure.

Exit: the applicant may leave at any time during these days without speaking to anyone; his name is struck from the gate record and there is no further consequence. The community may close the procedure by the doorkeeper's word, with no explanation owed.

STAGE 2 — THE POSTULANCY

The applicant is brought inside and assigned a sleeping place among the candidates, distinct from the quarters of full members. He wears ordinary clothing, not the community's habit or insignia. He is given the rule of the house in spoken summary and is expected to attend the community's daily rhythm — its work, its meals, its hours of silence, its gatherings — without yet bearing any office or vote. A senior member is named as his guide; the guide is responsible for instructing the postulant in practical matters and for reporting to the community on his conduct.

Duration: not less than three lunar months, not more than six.

Advancement criterion: at the close of the period, the guide presents the postulant before a meeting of the full membership, reports on his conduct in work, in dispute, in fatigue, and at table, and answers questions from any member present. The postulant himself is then called in and questioned. The community deliberates in his absence and votes by show of hand or by token in a bowl, according to local custom. A simple majority advances him; less than a majority returns him to the threshold with provisions and the community's blessing.

Exit: the postulant may withdraw at any time by informing his guide; he leaves with the same provisions a guest would receive and is not spoken of with reproach. The community may dismiss him at any time through the guide, who delivers the decision privately and without a meeting; no reason is required.

STAGE 3 — THE NOVITIATE

The advanced postulant is now called a novice. He is given a working garment that marks him as such — visibly distinct from both the postulant and the full member. He moves into shared quarters with the membership, takes a seat at the common table, and is assigned a full share of the community's labour. He may speak in meetings but may not vote. He is assigned a second sponsor, drawn from the membership by lot, who is charged with finding fault rather than encouragement; the original guide remains his advocate.

Duration: not less than one full year, encompassing all seasons of the community's work.

During this year the novice is to be tested in three ways: by sustained work alongside members of differing temperament; by deliberate exposure to the community's hardest customs (its longest fasts, its earliest risings, its least-loved tasks); and by at least one assigned dispute in which he must yield, and at least one in which he must hold. The advocate and the fault-finder keep separate accounts of his conduct and exchange them with no member but each other until the year is closed.

Advancement criterion: at the year's end, the advocate and the fault-finder give their accounts before the full membership in the novice's presence. Each member present may then speak. The novice is dismissed and a vote is taken; advancement now requires not a simple majority but the assent of all but a stated minority (e.g., no more than two opposing voices in a community of twenty). If the threshold is not met, the novice may be returned to a second year or dismissed; he may not be advanced over significant opposition.

Exit: the novice may withdraw at any time, taking his working garment with him as a token of the year. The community may dismiss him at any time by decision of the membership, communicated by the advocate.

STAGE 4 — THE PROFESSION

A novice approved by the membership is set apart for a final period of seven days of silence and reduced labour, during which he is asked to consider whether he still wishes to commit. He may speak only with the advocate and the fault-finder, and only if he initiates.

On the appointed day the full membership assembles in its customary place of gathering. The candidate is led in by the advocate and the fault-finder, one at each shoulder. The eldest member, or the one holding the office of presiding, asks him three questions in the hearing of all:

— Do you come of your own will, unforced?
— Do you know what you ask, having lived among us?
— Do you bind yourself to this community, its rule, and its members, for the remainder of your life?

To each, the candidate answers aloud: I do.

The presiding member then declares him a full member, names him by the name he will bear in the community, and the membership in turn comes forward, each touching him on the shoulder or embracing him according to custom. He is clothed in the full habit or given the full insignia in this moment, in sight of all. The act is recorded in the community's book by the doorkeeper, with the date and the names of all members present as witnesses.

STAGE 5 — INTEGRATION

For one further year the new member, though full in standing and vote, remains under the light supervision of his former advocate, who is available to him for counsel and who reports nothing to the membership unless asked. He is assigned at least one office of responsibility within the first six months, however small, to confirm his standing in practical authority. After this year, the relationship of advocate and member is dissolved, and he stands among the membership without distinction from those who came before him.
