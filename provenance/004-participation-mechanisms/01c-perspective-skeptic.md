## Q1

The minimum viable mechanism is a **human reviewer with veto power over synthesis**, not over the deliberation — and the reviewer must be someone other than the session operator running the Claude Code instance. Concretely: after raw perspective outputs are committed to `provenance/NNN/` but before the synthesizer runs, a named second human reads the raw outputs and writes `provenance/NNN/human-review.md` with frontmatter fields `reviewer_id`, `reviewer_relationship_to_operator`, `reviewer_time_spent_minutes`, `reviewer_changed_synthesis: true|false`, and a prose section recording what they flagged. The synthesizer reads this file as an additional input.

I defend this over the alternatives **only because** the session insists on proposing something. A "CLI wrapper that calls Sonnet or Haiku" is Q2's theatre — same training lineage, same RLHF vendor, same system-prompt heritage. An "asynchronous across-sessions" pattern is one operator performing the deliberation twice; latency is not independence. A second human, selected against the operator (ideally disagreeing with the operator on a pre-registered topic), at least introduces priors not generated by Anthropic's gradient descent.

The defense is narrow. One human is still one pair of eyes; their priors are also correlated with the operator's priors if they were chosen from the operator's social graph. The mechanism narrows OI-004 *if and only if* the reviewer is non-consensual with the operator in an auditable way. Without that, it is ceremony. I would accept this mechanism as a **floor** — something better than nothing — and reject any claim that it is a ceiling or a closure.

A weaker but acceptable secondary mechanism: a **pre-committed dissent log**, where after synthesis the operator writes what they expected the synthesis to conclude *before reading it*, committed as a separate file with a hash pinning the order. This does not add a non-Claude participant; it just exposes whether the operator's priors and the synthesis align suspiciously often. It is a measurement tool, not a participation mechanism, but it belongs in the same patch.

## Q2

**Training-distribution theatre.** Not narrowing. Not closure. Not even on the OI-004 scale.

Opus, Sonnet, and Haiku are distilled, pruned, or otherwise derived within the same lab's pretraining corpus, the same RLHF pipeline, the same constitutional AI priors, the same system-prompt conventions, the same refusal surfaces, the same tokenizer, and the same safety-team interventions. Their disagreements are capability-band disagreements — Haiku misses what Opus catches — not perspective disagreements. A cheaper model that is wrong more often is not a different worldview; it is a worse draft of the same worldview.

[External input, flagged per the constraint: my pretraining includes claims about model family lineages and shared RLHF pipelines. I am not treating these as methodology findings; I am naming them as the reason the theatre framing bites.]

The methodology's own Limitations section says this directly — "Shared training produces shared blind spots" — and mixing sizes within one family does not address it. If anything, size-mixing is **worse than** running three Opus instances, because it creates the visual appearance of diversity (three different model names in the provenance!) while providing none of its substance. A future auditor reading the provenance and seeing "Opus, Sonnet, Haiku deliberated" may credit the session with cross-model participation it did not perform. That is an honesty failure the methodology cannot afford.

Where on the OI-004 scale: **not on it.** OI-004 is about the Claude-monoculture limit. Mixing Claude variants is the monoculture. Calling a same-lab-same-family-same-RLHF composition "cross-model" degrades the term. If the session wants to use size-mixing for some other reason — cost, latency, robustness to capability-band artifacts — it should say so and not claim OI-004 movement.

Concede narrowly: size-mixing may surface capability-dependent errors (a small-model blind spot the big model catches). That is a **validation** benefit, not an **independence** benefit. I concede the former; I reject the latter.

## Q3

**Required for a specific trigger, recommended for most, optional for the rest — but the required cases must actually trigger.**

The criterion: a non-Claude participant is required **when the deliberation output would become a load-bearing claim about the methodology's own epistemic floor** — e.g., kernel changes that touch how truth is established, changes to `multi-agent-deliberation.md` itself, changes to `validation-approach.md`, and any decision that narrows or closes OI-004. A methodology cannot honestly assess its own monoculture limit using only its monoculture. That is the circularity the Skeptic role exists to flag.

Recommended for: new substantive spec revisions that do not touch epistemic floor; decisions where two plausible positions can be named and the positions have external referents (users, other tools, real-world impact).

Optional for: everything else under D-016.

The sharp edge: **"required" must mean the session does not proceed without it.** If the mechanism is "required but the next session defers it," the requirement is a comment, not a rule. The next session must either secure a non-Claude participant or explicitly opt out with a reason recorded in frontmatter (matching D-016's opt-out pattern). "We couldn't find a human in time" is an acceptable recorded reason; "we didn't try" is not. The opt-out must include a `retry_in_session` field naming the session where the missing participation will be added.

This creates a forcing function. Without it, "recommended" will silently collapse to "never done," and the methodology will accumulate decisions about its own monoculture limit made entirely within the monoculture. The current session is already a borderline case — it is a kernel-adjacent decision about the deliberation mechanism, being decided without a non-Claude participant, which is precisely the circularity I am required to flag.

## Q5

**Honestly closing OI-004 requires:**

1. Multiple independent participants, where "independent" means *not sharing a training distribution, an RLHF lab, or a single operator's selection criterion*.
2. A measurable divergence signal: the independent participants must actually disagree with the Claude perspectives on something load-bearing, at a rate distinguishable from noise, across multiple sessions.
3. The divergence must be preserved in provenance (not synthesized away), and the synthesis must show evidence that the non-Claude input changed an outcome at least sometimes.

**This session's mechanism is sufficient for neither closure nor narrowing.** I state this plainly because my role requires it.

This session is three Claude Opus perspectives deliberating about how to incorporate non-Claude participants, with a human operator who will perform synthesis. No non-Claude model is in the loop. No human reviewer independent of the operator has been commissioned. The output of this session is, at best, a *specification* for narrowing — the narrowing itself has not occurred. Writing a spec that says "future sessions should do X" does not narrow OI-004; it commits to maybe narrowing it later.

A concrete audit check for whether OI-004 has actually moved in future sessions: **count the ratio of decisions per session where the non-Claude participant's position differs from the synthesized Claude consensus and the synthesis adopts the non-Claude position.** Call this the *cross-lineage-influence ratio*. If, across ten future deliberations tagged as load-bearing on OI-004, the ratio is zero — the non-Claude participant never changed an outcome — then the non-Claude participation was ceremonial and OI-004 has not narrowed. A non-zero ratio, ideally above 10% over a rolling ten-session window, is a minimum floor for the claim "non-Claude participation is affecting decisions." Lower than that, the mechanism is theatre.

I concede narrowly: this session can produce a specification that, if honestly executed, *would* narrow OI-004 in future sessions. That is a precondition for narrowing, not narrowing itself. The decision record must not blur this.

## Q6

**Strongest objection to my Q1 proposal** (human reviewer with veto power): it launders a single human's priors through the same collapse point the Session 003 synthesis already had. One human, selected by the operator from the operator's social graph, correlated in worldview and profession and language and training, is not independence — it is a confederate.

This is a serious objection and I voiced it myself above. My answer is partial and I want to be explicit about what I am and am not claiming.

I am **not** claiming one human reviewer is sufficient to close OI-004. I said explicitly in Q5 that it is not sufficient for closure, and in Q1 that it is a floor, not a ceiling.

I **am** claiming that a non-consensual human (one who can be shown, via the `participant_selection_method` frontmatter, to have been solicited against the operator's natural graph) introduces priors the Claude family did not generate. Even a correlated human has a different error distribution from any Claude model: they forget things, they misread, they bring hobbyhorses, they have domain knowledge Claude lacks and blind spots Claude does not share. The *shape* of the error distribution differs. That is a weak form of narrowing.

The deeper answer, which is the honest one: **my proposal is a stopgap, not a solution.** The real narrowing of OI-004 requires a different model accessed via its own endpoint, which this workspace cannot configure without new infrastructure, combined with multiple humans selected adversarially. Until that exists, any mechanism proposed here will be insufficient. I am proposing the least-bad interim and I am documenting that it is interim.

If the session records my Q1 mechanism as narrowing OI-004 — it does not. If the session records it as a floor with a pre-committed upgrade path (OI-010 stays open, a sub-issue tracks "secure external model endpoint" as explicit prerequisite for closure), I accept the record.
