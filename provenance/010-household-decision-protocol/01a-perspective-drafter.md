## Q1. Shape

A **one-page document with four or five named moves**, laid out as short headed sections with a line or two of prose under each. Not a numbered list (too procedural), not a flow diagram (too engineered), not a cyclic sequence (implies a repeating meta-practice the couple hasn't signed up for).

The reason is almost entirely about how it sounds when read aloud. A flow diagram reads as "process". A numbered list of steps reads as "instructions". Headed short sections read as "a note someone wrote for us" — which is the register we want.

I'd call the moves something like:

- **Opening the question**
- **What we each care about**
- **What we're actually choosing between**
- **Deciding**
- **(optional) Checking back in**

Gerunds, not imperatives. "Opening the question" invites; "Open the question" commands. Morning Unfurl's aesthetic is practical but invitational, and gerunds are load-bearing in that register.

One page. Two at the absolute outside, only if "Checking back in" earns a second page. The document should feel like something you'd magnet to the fridge, not something you'd file.

Load-bearing position: **the shape is subordinate to the voice.** If we get the voice right, almost any shape works; if we get the voice wrong, no shape rescues it. So I'm picking the shape that puts the fewest obstacles between the reader and warm prose.

## Q2. Content

Four moves, with an optional fifth. Explicit in the artefact; everything else left to the couple.

**1. Opening the question.** One of us names that there's a decision worth sitting down for, and roughly what it's about. This move exists because the alternative is the decision getting made accidentally over three weeks of half-conversations. Draft copy:

> "One of us says: *I think this is worth actually deciding on.* The other says yes or not yet. If not yet, name a when."

The "not yet" clause matters. It refuses the frame that the initiator's timing is the correct timing. It costs three words.

**2. What we each care about.** Before we talk options, we each say — briefly — what we want this decision to do for us. Not positions ("I want the oak shelves"), stakes ("I want the lounge to feel like a room we read in, not a room we store things in"). This is the only move that does emotional work explicitly, and it does it by asking for it once, early, plainly, and then moving on.

Draft copy:

> "Before we talk options, each of us says what we want this decision to do for us — the feeling or function, not the thing. One sentence is enough. Two if one sentence won't do it."

"One sentence is enough" is doing real work. It sets a ceiling on this move so it doesn't become a therapy turn.

**3. What we're actually choosing between.** We name two or three real options. Not a brainstorm. Not every possibility. The live ones. If one of us has a strong preference already, that's fine — it goes in the list and the others join it.

Draft copy:

> "We write down the actual options — two or three. Not every possibility, just the ones in play. If one of us is already leaning hard, that option goes in and the others join it."

**4. Deciding.** We decide. No voting protocol, no scoring, no rubric. The protocol's job is to have gotten us to this point with shared information; the deciding itself is left to us.

Draft copy:

> "We pick one. If one of us cares much more than the other, that usually settles it. If we both care and disagree, we either keep talking or sleep on it. No other machinery."

"No other machinery" is the sentence I'd fight for. It tells the reader: we respect you enough not to automate this part.

**5. (optional) Checking back in.** Some weeks later, a light check: did it do what we wanted? This is explicitly optional and explicitly short. Not a retrospective. Not a review.

Draft copy:

> "A few weeks in, one of us can ask: *is it doing what we wanted?* If yes, good. If no, we haven't failed — we've just learned something about the next one."

**Left to judgment, not specified:**

- How long any of this takes.
- Where it happens. (Kitchen table, walk, bed — not our business.)
- What tools, if any. (Paper, phone notes, nothing — not our business.)
- How much research happens between moves 2 and 3.
- Whether to involve others (friends, tradespeople) for input.
- What counts as "worth sitting down for". The couple will calibrate.

The principle: **specify the moves, not the choreography.**

## Q3. Register and tone

This is the question I care most about, so I'll be concrete.

**Person and voice.** First-person plural throughout — "we", "us", "our". Not "you and your partner". Not "the initiator" and "the other party" (those labels belong to the meta-brief, not the artefact). "We" positions the reader as already inside the couple the document is for; "you" positions the document as coming from somewhere outside and instructing.

The one exception: the opening line and any framing line can address the reader as "you" briefly, to establish that this was written *for* them, not *by* them:

> "Here's something to try if you and your person have a decision to make together and it's worth more than five minutes but less than a weekend."

"Your person" is the phrase I'd argue for hardest. It's warmer than "partner", less specific than "spouse", and — critically — it's what people actually say in their heads.

**Mood.** Invitational, not imperative. Verbs like *say*, *name*, *write down*, *pick* — not *communicate*, *articulate*, *document*, *select*. The corporate verbs sound like procurement policy, as the stance note warned. The plain verbs sound like a friend.

**Humour and warmth.** Warmth goes in the *asides* — the parenthetical or the half-sentence after the move. Humour, sparingly, only where it lands on the side of generosity (to the reader, to the couple). Not self-deprecating ("this protocol probably won't work but..."). Not knowing ("we all know how this usually goes..."). Just warm.

Example of warmth in an aside:

> "No other machinery. (If you want a spreadsheet, that's fine, but it's not the protocol's job to give you one.)"

That parenthetical is generous — it refuses to scold the reader who likes spreadsheets — while still holding the line.

**Register-markers that would be harmful.** I'll name them because they will creep in if we're not vigilant:

- Anything with "stakeholders", "alignment", "action items", "decision-maker", "criteria", "trade-offs", "pros and cons". Procurement register.
- Anything with "check in with yourself", "honor your needs", "hold space". Therapy register.
- Anything with "partner" used as a verb. "Let's partner on this decision." Cursed.
- Bullet points with bold lead-words followed by colons, like "**Listen:** take turns...". This is blog-advice register and it flattens everything.
- The word "framework". Anywhere.
- "Best practices". Anywhere.
- Em-dashes used to simulate thoughtful cadence in sentences that aren't actually thoughtful. (Use them when they earn their keep.)

**Sentence rhythm.** Mostly short. Occasional longer sentence for variation. No sentence should contain three ideas. The reader is trying to do something with their household; they don't need to do syntactic parsing.

**Title.** Not "Household Decision-Making Protocol". Not "The Two-Person Decision Framework". I'd propose something like:

> **"Deciding Together"**

or, if the user wants a touch more specific:

> **"A way of deciding, for two"**

The second has Morning Unfurl's lilt. It names the thing without inflating it.

## Q4. Handling emotional stakes

The protocol should acknowledge uneven stakes *once*, plainly, in the language of the move itself, and then trust the reader. It should not have a dedicated "emotions" section, a "check in on how you're feeling" step, or any instrumentation around affect.

The acknowledgment belongs inside Move 4 (Deciding):

> "If one of us cares much more than the other, that usually settles it."

That single sentence does the whole job. It says: (a) we know caring is uneven, (b) that unevenness is useful information, not a problem, (c) we trust you to notice it. No step required.

**What it must not do:**

- No "rate your emotional investment 1–10". Obviously. But also: no soft version of this, like "notice how much this matters to each of you". The "notice" phrasing is therapy register smuggling in the same instrument.
- No "make sure both voices are heard". This is corporate-diversity register and it's also insulting to a couple who know each other.
- No "take turns". The couple knows how to take turns. Specifying it implies they don't.
- No "acknowledge your partner's feelings". Scold register.
- No conflict-resolution scaffolding of any kind. The brief is explicit: assume trust. The protocol should *read* as assuming trust, which means not having machinery that would only be needed if trust were absent.
- No reference to "compromise". It's a loaded word and it frames every decision as a loss. A better frame, already in the moves: we each say what we want the decision to do for us, and then we decide.

The deeper principle: **emotional texture shows up in the protocol's generosity toward the reader, not in dedicated emotion-handling steps.** The parenthetical that refuses to scold the spreadsheet-liker, the "one sentence is enough" ceiling, the "no other machinery" refusal — these do the emotional work. They communicate "we're not going to make this weird". That's the emotional promise the couple is looking for.

## Q5. What the protocol refuses

I'll name four, with reasons, drawing from the brief's menu.

**1. Voting thresholds.** There are two people. Voting is either 1–1 (deadlock, no mechanism) or 2–0 (consensus, no vote needed). Any voting language imports a register where voting is a real tool, which for a dyad it isn't. Refusing voting also refuses the frame that the protocol's job is to arbitrate between two adversaries. It isn't.

**2. Weighted scoring matrices.** A scoring matrix for $500 shelving decisions is absurd on its face, and the absurdity is instructive: it tells you the protocol's ambitions. If we include scoring, we've written a decision-support tool. If we refuse scoring, we've written something that respects the reader's own judgment. The refusal should appear in the "what this refuses" section verbatim:

> "No scoring matrices. If you want to make a spreadsheet, go ahead, but this isn't one."

**3. Mandatory cool-off periods.** The "sleep on it" option appears in Move 4, but as an option, not a requirement. A mandated cool-off period reads as distrust: *you two can't be trusted to decide in the moment*. The couple is described as emotionally intelligent and psychologically secure; the protocol should act like it believes that.

**4. Post-decision review.** The optional check-in (Move 5) is already pushing at the edge of what the artefact can hold without tipping into retrospective register. A *mandatory* post-decision review would be the tipping point. "Review" is a word from the world of meetings. The check-in as written is a curious question a few weeks later, not an agenda item.

I'd let the "what this refuses" section do the same work Morning Unfurl's does — short, direct, slightly wry. Candidate wording for the section itself:

> **What this isn't**
>
> - Not a voting system. There are two of you.
> - Not a scoring matrix. Make a spreadsheet if you want, but that's not this.
> - Not a cool-off requirement. Sleep on it if you want to. Don't if you don't.
> - Not a review process. The check-in is a question, not a meeting.

The heading "What this isn't" rather than "What this refuses" — because "refuses" has become methodology-vocabulary and the artefact shouldn't wear its methodology on its sleeve. "What this isn't" is what a person would actually say.

## Q6. Meta / honest reaction

Two flags, one request.

**Flag 1: the word "protocol" itself.** It's in the problem statement and it's in the session's shared vocabulary, but it's exactly the kind of word that will push the downstream writing in a corporate direction. "Protocol" has connotations of medicine, diplomacy, IT, and meeting etiquette — all wrong registers. If the final artefact is titled or introduced as a "protocol", we've already lost a chunk of the battle. I'd argue for the word "way" or "approach" in the artefact itself ("a way of deciding, for two"), reserving "protocol" for internal methodology language only. This matters enough that I'd flag it as a register hazard even if the rest of the deliberation is settled.

**Flag 2: "decision-making" as a hyphenated compound.** Same problem, smaller scale. "Decision-making" is a business-school noun phrase. "Deciding" is a verb people use. Wherever possible in the artefact, convert the noun phrase to the verb. "Deciding together", not "Joint decision-making".

**Request: don't over-specify Move 5.** The optional check-in is the most dangerous move in terms of register drift. Every instinct in a design process is to make it more concrete, give it a template, specify a timeframe. Every one of those moves would push the artefact toward corporate. The check-in has to stay one sentence, one suggestion, explicitly optional, explicitly light. If synthesis pressures this move to be more specified, I'd push back.

**On the methodology's domain-general claim.** No substantive concern. The Morning Unfurl precedent suggests the methodology handles small, domestic, validated-by-recipient artefacts well. A two-person household decision protocol is squarely in that territory — it's a small artefact with one validating couple. The risk isn't the domain; the risk is the vocabulary surrounding the methodology itself ("protocol", "deliberation", "kernel", "artefact") leaking into the artefact. The artefact has to be written as if the methodology didn't exist. That's the craft discipline for this session.
