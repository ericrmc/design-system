## Q1. Shape

A **short list of named moves with a clear sequence, plus one explicit loop-back**. Not a cyclic flow, not a decision tree, not a two-column dialogue, not a ritual set.

Reasoning against the context:

- **Linear list** wins because two people, one decision, needs a clear front and back. A cyclic flow suggests the decision keeps returning; it doesn't — the decision gets made and the house changes. A cycle would encode "we'll revisit this" into the shape, which misrepresents the problem.
- **Decision tree** fails because the branching isn't on external conditions — it's on the two people's preferences, which the protocol shouldn't pre-enumerate. Trees read as corporate the moment they exist.
- **Two-column dialogue layout** is seductive because it's symmetric, but it forces every step into parallel simultaneous motion. Several moves here are genuinely sequential (one person names the decision first; both people weigh in; one person calls the close). A column layout would lie about that.
- **Ritual set** (unordered "moves") abandons the failure mode the user named as active — *the process drifts because no one owns the "decide now" moment*. Unordered moves have no close.
- **One-page checklist** is the closest cousin. The difference I'm drawing: a checklist implies you tick each item off in isolation. What I want is named steps where each step is a short paragraph describing a small action, not a checkbox.

The **one loop-back**: after both people have said what they want, before the close, there's an explicit beat for "is there something underneath this we haven't said?" If yes, loop back to naming. If no, proceed to close. This is the only non-linearity, and it exists because *surface criteria mask real stakes* is a named failure mode.

Format: ~1 printed page, 5 or 6 named steps, each with a 2–3 sentence gloss. Matches the "Morning Unfurl" reference in density.

## Q2. Content

Five steps. Each one earns its place by addressing a specific failure mode from my role brief.

**1. Name the decision out loud, and who's bringing it up.**
*Addresses: one party's preferences compound because they articulate first.*
The initiator writes or says one sentence: what are we deciding, and roughly what's the budget and timeframe. The protocol names that whoever brings it up goes first in step 2 — not because they get more weight, but because making this visible defuses the hidden advantage of speaking first. "I'm bringing this up" is the honest frame.

**2. Each person says what they want, uninterrupted.**
*Addresses: both parties under-state to be agreeable and end up with a compromise neither wants; one party silently stockpiles disagreement.*
Each person says what they'd pick if it were up to them alone, and why. Not a negotiated middle ground. Not a hedge. The actual preference. The other person doesn't respond yet. The "uninterrupted" marker matters because the failure mode is *real-time adjustment of one's stated preference in response to the other's face* — that's where the under-statement happens.

**3. Say the thing underneath, if there is one.**
*Addresses: the decision is made on surface criteria while real stakes sit underneath.*
One beat for "is there something about this decision that matters to me that I haven't said?" Not required to produce something. The question being asked at all is the move. Possible undersides named as examples, not as a list to complete: routine disruption, symbolism, whose domain of the house this touches, how long you expect to live with it.

**4. Decide who cares more, and whether that settles it.**
*Addresses: uneven stakes; decision paralysis.*
The explicit question: does one of us clearly care more about this than the other? If yes, the one who cares less says so, and that may be enough to decide. If no, or if the one who cares more wants the other's genuine buy-in anyway, proceed to step 5. This step is where I'm taking the strongest position — see Q4.

**5. Call it, or name what's still in the way.**
*Addresses: the process drifts because no one owns the "decide now" moment.*
Either: one person says "I think we've decided — we're going with X, yes?" and the other confirms. Or: one person names what's still unresolved ("I want to see the shelving in person before we commit"). If unresolved, the protocol says when you'll come back to it — a specific day, not "soon". The close is owned by someone saying it out loud.

**(Optional sixth: a short after-note, weeks later.)** I'm ambivalent. A post-decision check-in addresses *resentment shows up months later* but risks corporate-review-meeting register. I'd include it as a single sentence at the bottom: "A few weeks in, it's worth one sentence each on whether this still feels right." Not a scheduled review. Not a form. Just a named possibility.

**What I'm deliberately leaving to judgment (not in the artefact):**
- How long the conversation takes.
- Where it happens (kitchen, walk, couch).
- Whether to write anything down.
- How to phrase the opening.
- What counts as "caring more" — not quantified.
- Whether to involve the decision object physically (looking at shelves together, walking the side of the house).

Over-specifying any of these would tip the document into prescriptive territory and break the "household-native" constraint.

## Q3. Register and tone

**Addressing the reader:** second person plural — "you two" or just "you" in a way that reads as addressing both. Not "the parties". Not "the initiator and the respondent". I'd open with something like "This is for the two of you, when you're deciding something about the house that matters more than a grocery run and less than a move."

**Voice:** invitational, not imperative. "Say what you want" rather than "State your preference". "Call it" rather than "Confirm the decision". The imperative mood is fine for short, plain verbs; it becomes corporate the moment the verbs get Latinate ("articulate", "validate", "reconcile").

**First-person plural ("we") vs. second-person ("you"):** second-person addressing the pair. "We" in the document would implicate the author/methodology, which is wrong register for a household artefact. "You" addressed to the couple puts the document in the position of a friendly guide, not a participant.

**Where humour/warmth belongs:** in one or two specific asides, not as a through-line. The "Morning Unfurl" reference permits dry precision with occasional warmth. I'd put warmth in (a) the opening frame and (b) the "refuses" section. Elsewhere, plain and direct.

**Specific wording I'd propose verbatim:**
- Step 2: **"Each of you says what you'd actually pick."** The word "actually" is load-bearing — it names the failure mode (understatement) without lecturing.
- Step 3: **"Is there anything underneath this?"** Plain. Not "subtext", not "underlying concerns".
- Step 4: **"One of you might care more. That's allowed, and sometimes it's enough."** The phrase "that's allowed" is the household-native marker — it's the voice of a friend giving permission, not a framework granting it.
- Step 5: **"Someone has to say 'we've decided'."** Not "a decision will be confirmed". The word "someone" is important — the protocol doesn't pre-assign who closes.

**Register-markers that would be harmful:**
- Any word ending in -ization, -ification, -ment used as jargon.
- Bullet point symbols. Numbered steps are fine; bullets within steps read as slides.
- The word "stakeholder". The word "alignment". The word "framework" referring to itself.
- Headers with colons and capitalized terms ("Step 1: Preference Articulation").
- Any reference to "best practices" or "proven approaches".
- Present-tense third-person descriptions of what the protocol does ("This protocol helps couples..."). The document should address the reader, not describe itself.

**One warmth-marker I'd use:** the document names that it's a document. Something like "This is a short thing you can both read in two minutes." Meta-acknowledgment that it's paper defuses the process-document atmosphere.

## Q4. Handling emotional stakes

This is where I'm taking the strongest position.

**The core move: make unevenness sayable.** The protocol should explicitly name that two people almost never care equally about a given decision, and that the caring-less party saying so is a legitimate way to decide. This is step 4's entire purpose.

The failure mode I'm designing against: *both parties under-state to be agreeable*. When two emotionally intelligent people share a household, they often both say "whatever you want" because each wants to give the other space. The result is a compromise neither chose. The protocol's counter-move is to ask **directly**: does one of you care more? If yes, the other one can say so, and the decision gets made. This isn't the caring-more party "winning" — it's the caring-less party *granting* the decision, which is a different gesture.

**What the protocol must not do:**

- **No "feelings check-in" language.** No "how are you feeling about this?" That's therapeutic register, wrong for the household. The protocol asks about preferences and stakes, not feelings about preferences.
- **No validation rituals.** "I hear you saying..." mirroring. "Let's honour both perspectives." These are facilitation imports that would break the register instantly. *(external-input: from the facilitation literature — naming so I avoid it, not so I use it.)*
- **No scoring unevenness.** "On a 1–10 scale, how much does this matter to you?" — this converts a qualitative question into a false quantitative one and invites gaming.
- **No diversity-training gestures.** "Acknowledge your partner's lived experience of the lounge." This is parody-adjacent. Don't do it.
- **No forced equalization.** The protocol must not imply that both people should care the same amount, or that unevenness is a problem to resolve. Unevenness is normal and often useful.
- **No therapy-adjacent framing of the decision as revealing deeper relationship dynamics.** The lounge shelving is the lounge shelving. If there's something underneath, step 3 surfaces it; the protocol doesn't mine for it.

**The "underneath" step (step 3) and its limits:** the question "is there something underneath this?" should be asked once, plainly, and the answer "no" should be accepted without follow-up. The protocol trusts the person who says no. If the failure mode is that people don't surface their real stakes, asking the question once cracks that open. Asking it twice would be interrogation.

**Asymmetry of initiator and responder:** the user noted he typically initiates and his wife responds. The protocol names both positions but doesn't assign different rights. The one asymmetry I'd build in: the initiator owns the opening (step 1) and either person can own the close (step 5). The closer is whoever says "we've decided" first. In practice this often isn't the initiator, and that's fine — it distributes a small piece of ownership to the other party without engineering it.

## Q5. What the protocol refuses

Choosing five.

**1. No voting thresholds or weighted scoring.** Two people can't meaningfully vote — it's either a tie or unanimous. And weighted scoring (1–10 mattering-scales, criteria matrices) converts household texture into spreadsheet texture and loses everything. The protocol refuses this because the answer to "how do we decide when we disagree" is *one of you cares more, or you keep talking*, not *we calculate*.

**2. No written justifications.** The protocol does not ask anyone to write down why they want what they want. Articulating preferences out loud is enough; requiring justification in writing would convert this into a document-producing process. The artefact is the protocol, not the output of the protocol.

**3. No time limits, no mandatory cool-off periods.** Some decisions resolve in ten minutes; some resolve across three conversations over a week. Imposing either a ceiling or a floor breaks the household-native feel. The step-5 ("when will you come back to it") clause is enough to prevent drift without prescribing pace.

**4. No explicit veto rights.** Calling something a "veto" imports adversarial framing that doesn't belong between two people who trust each other. If someone genuinely can't live with an outcome, step 3 or step 4 is where that surfaces ("this matters more to me than I first said"). The protocol achieves the function of veto without the word.

**5. No third-party arbitration or mandatory post-decision review.** No "if you can't agree, ask a friend". No scheduled review meeting six weeks later. Both would domesticate the protocol into something more apparatus-like than the decisions warrant. The optional one-sentence after-note (Q2) gestures at post-decision reflection without mandating it.

## Q6. Meta / honest reaction

Three things.

**One: the A$500 budget band is doing more work than the brief acknowledges.** At $500, the stakes are real enough to matter but low enough that the protocol can be short. If the protocol were being designed for a $20,000 decision (renovation, vehicle), I'd want different steps — more deliberate information-gathering, probably a sleeping-on-it beat, likely a revisit phase. The protocol I've described is calibrated to the specific band named. I'd flag in the artefact's "refuses" section that it's not built for larger decisions, so the couple doesn't over-apply it.

**Two: the domain-general claim is partially supported here.** This external application tests whether a text-based methodology for producing artefacts can produce a household-native artefact — a genre that's usually produced informally or not at all. I think it can, but the risk is that the methodology's own register (rigorous, specified, iterative) leaks into the artefact. The artefact has to shed the methodology's voice entirely. Session 008's success suggests this is possible; whether it happens here depends on whether the synthesis step actively *subtracts* methodology-voice from the output. I'd flag this as a real risk worth naming in synthesis.

**Three: a missing piece in the brief.** The brief describes the two people as "emotionally intelligent and psychologically secure" and says the protocol shouldn't *require* those qualities. But the protocol I've described does assume a baseline of honesty — step 2 ("say what you'd actually pick") and step 3 ("is there something underneath?") both depend on the two people telling each other the truth in a specific moment. That's not the same as emotional intelligence, but it's also not nothing. If there's a mode where one or both parties routinely soften-lie to the other, this protocol doesn't help and might make things worse by forcing the soft-lie into the open. I don't think that's the user's situation, but it's worth naming that the protocol assumes a modest floor of honesty-in-the-moment, distinct from psychological security.

No hazardous framings detected. The problem is well-posed.
