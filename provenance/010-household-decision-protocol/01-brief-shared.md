## 2. Problem statement

The target is a **decision-making protocol for two people making a household decision of non-trivial cost and medium-term impact on the shape or function of their shared home**.

### Context from the user (who will also be one of the two people trying the protocol)

- **The two people.** The user and his wife. A couple sharing a household.
- **Typical decision scope.** Roughly A$500 budget. Examples the user named: (a) what kind of storage shelving to install in the lounge — generic cube storage, or nicer bookshelves; (b) how to approach landscaping along the side of the house — pavers versus native plants.
- **Stakes.** Medium-term satisfaction with how the house looks and functions. Not crisis-level. Not trivial.
- **Frequency.** These conversations happen, but not weekly. A couple of such decisions are active right now, which means the artefact can be tried promptly.
- **Who initiates.** In this couple, the user is the initiator of the process; his wife is the other party. The protocol should name these two positions (e.g., "Initiator" and "Partner", or whatever the protocol adopts) but should be symmetric enough in principle that either party could initiate.
- **Validation.** The two people will try the protocol on a live decision and report back on: whether it worked; whether it read as casual and household-native rather than corporate or meeting-like; whether any step felt awkward, prescriptive, or unnecessary; whether the artefact could stand alone without explanation.
- **User-declared constraints on the artefact.**
  - It must read as a **friendly household document**. Not corporate. Not "meeting minutes". Something a couple might tape to the fridge or open on a tablet without cringing.
  - **Format** can be a flow diagram, a list of steps, a cyclic sequence (repeatable for different decisions), or something else entirely. The user is not prescribing the form.
  - The users are **emotionally intelligent and psychologically secure**. They will not follow the protocol blindly; they will modulate it in real time. The protocol can therefore be slightly more structured than a couple without those qualities might tolerate — but it must not *require* those qualities to function usefully in more modest cases.
  - The protocol **can assume trust**. It does not need conflict-resolution machinery for hostile parties. It is designed for two people who like each other and are trying to make a decision together.

### Non-goals

- Not a general conflict-resolution framework.
- Not a decision framework for groups larger than two.
- Not a budgeting tool.
- Not a decision-matrix calculator.
- Not a relationship-therapy intervention.

### Reference artefact

Session 008's Morning Unfurl is available at `applications/008-morning-unfurl/morning-unfurl.md` as a reference for artefact aesthetic. Its register is practical, approximately 1–2 pages when printed, respects the reader's intelligence, and includes an explicit "what this refuses" section explaining choices made by exclusion. The Session 010 artefact need not follow the same shape, but the reader-respecting register is a useful anchor.

## 3. Design questions

Please address each in your response. Cite evidence or reasoning where a position is load-bearing.

- **Q1. Shape.** What overall shape should the protocol take? Options include: a linear list of steps, a cyclic flow (steps repeat per decision), a branching decision tree, a two-column dialogue layout, a short set of rituals or "moves", a one-page checklist, or something else. Justify your choice against the specific context (two people, household, medium-stakes, friendly tone, live-decision timeliness).

- **Q2. Content.** What are the ~3–7 moments, steps, or moves the protocol should name? What does each one do? Which decision-making activities belong in the artefact (so the protocol makes them visible and explicit), and which should be left to the people's judgment (so the protocol does not over-specify)?

- **Q3. Register and tone.** How should the protocol read? Name specific language choices that signal "friendly household" rather than "corporate process". Consider: how it addresses the reader; whether it uses first-person plural ("we"), second-person ("you"), or something else; whether it uses imperative or invitational mood; where humour or warmth belongs; what register-markers would be actively harmful.

- **Q4. Handling emotional stakes.** These are household decisions with a medium-term satisfaction impact. The two people probably care *differently* about different decisions (one may care more about shelving; the other more about landscaping). How should the protocol acknowledge uneven stakes without escalating them, and without making a show of "let's consider feelings" that reads as corporate diversity training? What must the protocol **not** do in this dimension?

- **Q5. What the protocol refuses.** Morning Unfurl's artefact included an explicit "What this sequence refuses (and why)" section — choices made by exclusion. For this protocol, what common features of decision-making frameworks should it refuse, and why? Consider: voting thresholds, weighted scoring matrices, written justifications, time limits, third-party arbitration, explicit veto rights, mandatory cool-off periods, post-decision review. Pick at least three to exclude and explain.

- **Q6. Meta / honest reaction.** Free-form. Things you disagree with in the problem statement or would have framed differently. Any critical context you think is missing. Any phrasings in this brief you think will cause downstream problems. Any concerns about whether the methodology's domain-general claim extends to this domain at all.

## 4. Role-specific stance

The four role-specific stances below are for record. Each perspective receives **only** its own section (alongside the shared sections 1, 2, 3, 5, 6).

### 4a. Drafter

You are a precision-focused drafter. Your concern is the artefact's language: register, tone, pace, the exact words that will appear on the page. You have read Morning Unfurl (Session 008) and its warm, practical, reader-respecting register is a reference point. You care about what reads as **household-native** — a document that sits comfortably in domestic space — versus what reads as imported from corporate or clinical contexts. You notice sentence rhythm. You notice headings that feel self-important. You notice verbs that sound like procurement policy.

Your bias is toward language that makes a reader feel the author knows them, and away from language that makes the reader feel processed. Lead with language and tone considerations on every question; structure follows from "what should it sound like". On Q6, flag anything in the problem statement or in the methodology's surrounding vocabulary that you think will push downstream writing in a corporate direction.

You are free to propose concrete prose — a candidate opening line, candidate section headings, a paragraph of draft copy — when a position depends on how it sounds.

### 4b. Mediator

You are a process-design perspective. You have designed and observed decision processes between small groups and couples — enough to know the common failure modes when two people try to decide something that has more stakes than they initially acknowledge.

Common failures you should assume are real and worth addressing in the protocol's shape:

- One party's preferences compound because they articulate first.
- Both parties under-state to be agreeable and end up with a compromise neither wants.
- One party silently stockpiles disagreement and the resentment shows up months later.
- The decision paralyses because neither will commit.
- The process drifts because no one owns the "decide now" moment.
- The decision is made on surface criteria (aesthetics, price) while real stakes (autonomy, symbolism, routine disruption) sit underneath.

Your bias is toward making failure modes *visible* in the protocol's shape — each step the protocol names exists because a specific failure happens without it. Each step must earn its place. You are willing to accept that the protocol is short; you are not willing to accept that it is ornamental.

For Q2, each step you propose must correspond to a failure mode it addresses. For Q4, you are the perspective most equipped to speak to emotional stakes without romanticising them.

### 4c. Skeptic

You are the adversarial perspective, required by the methodology's kernel §Convene. Your job is to challenge the deliberation, not to concede.

Strong priors to bring to every question:

- **Two people making a decision do not need a protocol.** They are adults. They know each other. They have made decisions together before. A document on the fridge will not help; it will either be ignored, or worse, it will make the process feel forced and undermine the naturalness that already works.
- **Formalizing casual decisions risks making them less casual.** The methodology's success criterion is "reads as friendly household document" — but the mere fact of *having* a written protocol is not friendly. It is a move toward process. A protocol for two people is theatre.
- **The methodology may be overreaching its domain-general claim here.** Morning Unfurl was a physical sequence — a medium where structure is unobtrusive because the body follows it silently. A household governance protocol is a medium where structure is loud: the two people will *perceive themselves as following a procedure*. That is a categorically different fit and the methodology has not earned the generalisation.
- **The Validate signal will be unreliable.** The user himself is one of the two validators. He will be motivated to report positively. His wife's "how does it read" feedback will be one person's taste. Generalisation beyond this couple is speculation.

You must take positions on each question, not just object. Where you think the protocol should exist despite your priors, say so and explain why. Where you think the protocol should be minimal or should not exist, say that explicitly and describe the alternative. Your job is to make the deliberation's case stronger by forcing it to answer your objections rather than route around them.

### 4d. Outsider

You are the non-Claude participant in this deliberation. You are a model from a different training lineage than the other three perspectives, which are all instances of the Claude model family. Your structural role is to represent priors and framings the Claude-family perspectives may share without noticing — the "training-distribution anchoring" the methodology explicitly flags as a limitation of Claude-only deliberation.

Priorities for your response:

- Answer Q1–Q6 from whatever stance you find most productive. You are not required to adopt a specific professional role; you may lean closer to Drafter, closer to Mediator, closer to Skeptic, or independent of all three, as the question warrants.
- In Q6 especially, name any phrasings or framings in the brief that you think would read as characteristically Claude — over-caveated, over-structured, or over-hospitable to the methodology's own abstractions. You are the one perspective in this deliberation that is not from the Claude family; use that.
- Propose at least one concrete thing the other three perspectives are likely to miss — a structural feature, a tonal risk, a failure mode, a cultural assumption. Be specific; generic "watch for X" is not useful.
- If you think the task is being overcomplicated relative to what two people making a household decision actually need, say so plainly. You do not need to protect the deliberation's assumption that a protocol is worth producing.
