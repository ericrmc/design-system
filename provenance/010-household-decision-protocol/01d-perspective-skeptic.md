## Q1. Shape

No protocol. Or if a protocol must ship, the smallest possible object: a single index card of prompts, not a procedure.

Start with the load-bearing objection. These two people have made household decisions together for however long they have been a household. The storage-shelving and the landscaping are not the first A$500 decisions they have faced. Whatever they do when these decisions come up — talk about it over dinner, look at photos on a phone, circle back a week later — already works well enough that the user has never before felt the need to design a protocol for it. The methodology is now proposing to formalise a process that has been running un-formalised for years. The burden of proof is on the artefact to demonstrate it adds something the absence of an artefact did not.

A **flow diagram** is the worst option. It imports visual grammar from process engineering into a context that has been explicitly designated non-corporate. Arrows and boxes do not become friendly because you draw them with rounded corners. A **branching decision tree** is worse still — it anticipates branch points the couple will discover for themselves, and it forces the eye to trace logic rather than read prose. A **cyclic flow** implies the steps repeat, which is a claim about the structure of household deliberation that has not been demonstrated. A **two-column dialogue layout** is the most corporate of all: it scripts roles, which is exactly the register hazard the user named.

If a protocol ships, the shape should be a **short prose page with three or four named prompts**, readable in under a minute, without arrows, without numbered mandatory steps, and without a visual structure that implies sequence is enforced. Think of the aesthetic of a recipe card or a hand-written note pinned to the inside of a cupboard — not a document. The word "protocol" should not appear in the artefact at all. Neither should "step", "phase", or "stage".

My preferred shape: **a page of three or four questions the two of them can work through in any order, with a one-line framing at the top**. That is less than a protocol. It is a conversation starter kit. I claim it is the most the situation can bear without becoming theatre.

## Q2. Content

Three prompts. Maybe four. Not seven. The Drafter will be tempted toward seven because seven feels complete; resist that.

The decision in front of this couple has, at minimum, these real moments: (a) one of them notices the decision needs making; (b) they each form a rough view; (c) they talk; (d) they pick something or defer. Everything else is scaffolding around those four, and most scaffolding is removable.

The prompts that earn their place:

1. **"What is this actually about?"** — because the shelving-versus-bookshelves decision is rarely only about shelving. It is about whether the lounge should look more like a library or more like a utility room; about whether one of them cares more about aesthetics in this room than the other; about whether the A$500 is money that would otherwise go somewhere. Naming the thing behind the thing is the single most useful move two people can make on a household decision, and they often skip it.

2. **"Who cares more about this one?"** — because the brief explicitly flags uneven caring as a real feature of these decisions, and surfacing it early is cheaper than discovering it mid-argument. This is the prompt that has to earn its keep against the corporate-gesture hazard. I think it can, if worded plainly. More on this in Q4.

3. **"What would we regret in a year?"** — medium-term satisfaction is the actual success criterion the user named. A regret check is more honest than a benefits check because it forces both people to imagine the outcome they do not want, which is the side of the trade-off that optimism underweights.

That is three. A fourth I would allow only if it is the simplest possible closing move:

4. **"What are we doing, and who's doing it?"** — a one-sentence landing, not a summary, not minutes. The purpose is not to document; it is to prevent the thing where one person thought the decision was made and the other thought it was still being discussed.

What does **not** belong in the artefact:

- A separate "research" or "information-gathering" step. These two people already know how to look up bookshelves. Specifying it would be patronising.
- A "define success criteria" step. Corporate tell.
- A budget step. The A$500 is a description of scope, not a thing the couple needs prompting to consider.
- A "feelings check-in". Register hazard, and the brief explicitly rejects therapy-adjacent moves.
- A "revisit this decision in N weeks" step. Post-decision review is institutional machinery.
- Any step that exists because the protocol would otherwise feel too short. Brevity is a feature.

The rule: every prompt must be one the couple plausibly would skip without the artefact, and whose skipping would cost them something. Prompts they would do anyway are padding. Prompts whose skipping costs nothing are padding. That is the test.

## Q3. Register and tone

The single most important register decision is **not writing the artefact in the voice of the artefact**. What I mean: most how-to documents speak *to* the reader — "first, you identify the decision to be made". The register hazard is highest exactly there. A friendly household document does not instruct.

Concrete choices I endorse:

- **First-person plural** for the prompts themselves. "What is this actually about?" reads as something the two of them might say to each other. "What is the decision about?" reads as something a facilitator would ask them. The difference is the entire register battle in miniature.
- **No addressing the reader.** No "you". No "the initiator" or "the other party" or "Partner A / Partner B". The user flagged that the protocol should name the two positions but be symmetric. I take the minimum interpretation of that: a one-line framing at the top that says something like *"one of us raises it, the other joins in"*, and after that the artefact speaks as "we".
- **No imperatives.** Not "discuss", not "consider", not "identify". The prompts are questions. Questions are invitational by grammatical default.
- **No warmth-signalling.** The register hazard goes both ways. Corporate is one failure mode; folksy is the other. No emoji, no exclamation marks, no "Hey! So you're making a decision together — fun!". The reference artefact (Morning Unfurl) is practical and respectful, not chummy. Match that.
- **The "what this refuses" section should be kept** but renamed so it does not read as meta-commentary on the protocol itself. Morning Unfurl could afford the meta-framing because a movement sequence has natural boundaries against which refusals read as practical. A decision protocol refusing things reads as a protocol being self-conscious about being a protocol, which is exactly the theatre I am warning against. I would rename it to something like **"Not this"** and list two or three things in a neutral voice.
- **Length.** Half a page if possible. One page maximum. The Morning Unfurl reference is 1–2 pages because movement sequences need more instruction per move than questions do. This artefact should be shorter than its reference, not equal to it.
- **Title.** Not "Household Decision Protocol". Not "Our Decision Process". Something that names the thing by its use, not its form. I do not have a strong candidate; the Drafter should propose one and the Mediator should stress-test it against the fridge-magnet standard.

Register-markers that would be **harmful**: any numbered checklist; any phrase including the words "stakeholder", "alignment", "outcome", "value"; any use of "partner" as a label rather than a relation; any visual frame that resembles a template; any "Congratulations! You've completed..." closing move.

## Q4. Handling emotional stakes

Uneven caring is real and the protocol should surface it in exactly one place — the "who cares more about this one?" prompt — and then let the humans handle it. Anything more is overreach.

The failure modes to avoid:

- **Corporate-diversity gesture.** Phrases like "honour each other's perspectives" or "ensure both voices are heard" are the exact language the brief tells us to avoid. They read as HR training because they are HR training. Strike on sight.
- **Therapeutic framing.** "Hold space for feelings", "check in with your emotional response", etc. The brief explicitly rules these out. The protocol is not a relationship intervention.
- **Over-naming.** Labelling emotional states ("you might feel frustrated when...") is patronising to psychologically secure adults and is also a register-marker for self-help literature.
- **Equalising machinery.** Anything that tries to balance stakes procedurally — "each person gets equal say", "weighted vote by intensity" — is a veto scheme dressed as fairness, and reintroduces the conflict-resolution machinery the brief rules out.

What the protocol **should** do: trust that two emotionally intelligent adults, once the question "who cares more about this one?" has been asked out loud, know what to do with the answer. The protocol's job is to make that question easier to ask, not to engineer what happens after.

One specific wording I would defend: the prompt should be **"who cares more about this one?"** with the trailing "this one" doing important work. It frames unevenness as situational — *this* decision, not *decisions in general* — which de-escalates by implication. "Who cares more about this?" without the "one" subtly invites comparison across decisions and has a scorekeeping undertone that the trailing word removes.

What the protocol must **not** do: suggest what to do when caring is uneven. No "the one who cares more should…", no "the one who cares less should…". That is the move from surfacing to prescribing, and prescribing is the line the brief draws.

## Q5. What the protocol refuses

I will name five refusals and argue each briefly.

**Refuse voting thresholds.** Two-person voting is either unanimity (which is just agreement) or tied (which is undecided). Thresholds are for groups of three or more. Proposing them for two people would be a methodology-grade error and would signal that the protocol was designed without thinking about n=2.

**Refuse weighted scoring matrices.** Matrices are decision-theory theatre for problems where the inputs are known and commensurable. Shelving versus bookshelves is not that problem. Reducing "how do we want the lounge to feel" to a weighted sum of attributes is the exact move that makes a casual decision feel corporate. This is one of the sharpest refusals to make because matrices are seductive: they look like rigour.

**Refuse written justifications.** If the artefact asks the couple to write down *why* they chose what they chose, it has become a documentation system. This is where protocols most often cross into meeting-minutes territory. The optional one-line landing in Q2 prompt 4 is the maximum allowable residue; anything more is archiving the marriage.

**Refuse third-party arbitration.** Obvious refusal but worth naming, because any hedge toward "if you can't agree, bring in someone else" signals the protocol does not trust its own premise. The brief stipulates trust; the artefact should behave as if it believed it.

**Refuse mandatory cool-off periods.** Time-boxing emotional process is itself an emotional gesture, and a patronising one. If the two of them need to sleep on it, they will sleep on it. The protocol does not need to authorise that.

Refusals I would consider but not include in a hard list:

- **Explicit veto rights** — tempting to refuse, but the "who cares more about this one?" prompt does implicit veto work by surfacing unevenness, and making veto explicit would be a formalisation step too far. The methodology should neither grant nor refuse explicit veto rights; it should simply not mention them.
- **Post-decision review** — I refused this implicitly in Q2 (no "revisit in N weeks" step), but I would not name it in the "not this" section because doing so would draw attention to a machinery the reader should never have considered in the first place.

The "not this" section should list probably three items, chosen to be the ones most likely to sneak back in during revision. My candidates: weighted scoring, written justifications, third-party arbitration. Voting thresholds do not need listing because they are too obviously wrong for two people; including them would make the artefact look like it was designed with three-person groups in mind and then retrofitted.

## Q6. Meta / honest reaction

The methodology is overreaching. Let me be specific.

The claim being tested in Session 010 is that a text-based design methodology that produced a successful physical-movement sequence (Morning Unfurl) can produce a successful household-governance artefact. These two media are categorically different in the way I flagged in the brief's skeptic priors: a movement sequence has structure the body follows silently, so formal structure does not collide with the experience of the activity. A decision protocol has structure the two people follow visibly, with full metacognitive awareness that they are following a procedure. That metacognition is the register hazard, and it is intrinsic to the medium, not fixable by tone.

I do not think Session 010 will cleanly falsify the methodology's domain-general claim, because the user will likely report that the artefact "worked" in some qualified sense. But I think Session 010's success, if achieved, will be **achieved against the medium** — that is, the best possible version of this artefact will succeed by being so light-touch that it barely functions as a protocol, and its success will be evidence that the methodology knows how to get out of its own way, not evidence that the methodology's process generalises to governance artefacts.

The deliberation should take this seriously because it changes what "good" looks like. Good is not a well-structured, well-shaped, complete decision process. Good is an artefact the couple tries once, finds mildly useful, and then internalises and no longer needs. The artefact should aim at its own obsolescence. A protocol that remains on the fridge after six months has failed, because it means the couple is still outsourcing something they should have absorbed.

Two concerns about the validation signal:

**The user is one of the two validators.** The brief acknowledges this. I want to name it harder: the user is also the person who commissioned the artefact, the person who will read the deliberation output before trying the artefact, and the methodology's primary author. The chain of motivated interpretation is long. The wife's reaction is the more signal-bearing half of the validation, but it is a sample of one person who has not seen the brief and whose aesthetic taste is not calibrated against the methodology's criteria. This is a fundamentally under-powered test, which is fine for a single session but should not be treated as a generalisable result.

**Consider a no-artefact control.** The methodology could commit to trying the next decision *without* the protocol and comparing. I do not expect this to happen — it contradicts the session's purpose — but I name it because its absence is a real gap in the validation design.

One hazardous framing in the problem statement worth flagging: the phrase "decision-making protocol" primes every downstream design choice toward formality. If the Drafter accepts the phrase, the artefact will drift corporate no matter how the tone is tuned. The shape question (Q1) is partly a question of whether we accept the frame. I am voting no.

Final position: ship the smallest possible artefact, treat the "worked" report with appropriate skepticism, and do not treat Session 010 as evidence the methodology generalises to governance-domain problems without at least one more external application in an adjacent domain (external-input flag: this is a general validity concern from experimental design, not from a specific source). The methodology's domain-general claim is worth testing, but testing it once with a favourably disposed couple is not a test.
