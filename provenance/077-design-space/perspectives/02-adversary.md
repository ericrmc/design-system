# adversary — blind position

## Frame

The brief in `constraints.md` reads as a single causal story: six properties of LLM agents compounded under a single-agent architecture using the wrong substrate, therefore the fix is multi-agent plus a database. That story is internally coherent and parts of it are almost certainly right. But it does two things a deliberation should not let go unchallenged. It reads a single seventy-five-session record as if it produced a unique diagnosis when at least three readings of the record fit the evidence. And the two "fixes" it commits to are load-bearing engineering bets the brief treats as the obvious next step rather than conjectures with their own predictable failure modes. My job is to make the brief earn its commitments rather than ratify them.

## Position

### The diagnosis is partial

`constraints.md` says the engine could no longer correct itself because slack disappeared, and slack disappeared because state accumulated past what one agent's working memory could hold (Property 4, Property 6). This is one reading. There is a second, simpler reading that the brief does not foreground: the methodology kept *adding* — engine-feedback pathways, retrieval substrate, close-time reviewer, layered audits — without ever subtracting, and the failure mode is "no one was responsible for removal" rather than "single agent holds too much." The methodology's own preservation list (`constraints.md` §What the methodology preserved) shows the engine was already a multi-perspective system by Session 4; multi-agent was not absent. What was absent was the authority to delete.

If the second reading is right, the fix is not "decompose the agent." The fix is the subtraction role plus a hard write-budget on the active spec surface, and the multi-agent decomposition is incidental at best, harmful at worst (more agents = more places ceremony can hide).

A third reading: self-hosting itself was the disease. The methodology survived contact with reality only on its early sessions, when the recursion was shallow. By Session 58 the engine was reasoning about the methodology of reasoning about methodology. `methodology.md` §Self-hosting concedes this: "self-development is the bootstrap, not the destination." If self-hosting is structurally recursive-trap-prone, no amount of agent decomposition will help — applying multi-agent decomposition to the methodology that produced the multi-agent decomposition is precisely the move that triggers the same trap one level up.

The deliberation should hold open whether the actual fix is a single move (subtraction + external problems) and whether the database and the multi-agent shape are responses to a misdiagnosis.

### The multi-agent shape carries pathologies the brief does not engage

`constraints.md` §Risks gestures at "coordination overhead, role-confusion, message-routing failures." This is a paragraph; it should be the design surface. Concrete pathologies a successor must distinguish from progress:

- **Orchestrator centralisation.** The brief proposes seven agent roles (reader, specifier, decider, deliberator group, reviewer, validator, assembler) coordinated through a substrate. The orchestrator that decides *which agent to invoke when* is itself a context-holder. If the orchestrator's prompt grows to encode the methodology's routing decisions, the orchestrator becomes the new single agent and the seven workers are appendages. Property 4 (foundational instructions decay under load) re-emerges in the orchestrator's prompt rather than in a worker's.
- **Coordination-via-substrate becomes coordination-via-prose-in-substrate.** Agents "talk to the substrate," but what they write is generated by models that default to prose (Property 1). A `decision` row carries a `body_md` column; a `deliberation` row carries a `synthesis_md`. The state that matters lives in those Markdown blobs, not in columns the validator can check. The substrate refuses *malformed* input; it cannot refuse *ceremonious* input.
- **The integration step is unspecified.** Multi-agent systems fail most often at integration, not at decomposition. If a worker assembles seven scoped contexts into the session artefact, the working-memory ceiling reappears in that worker. If the substrate does it, the schema must encode the full methodology and the database becomes the new monolith.
- **Distinguishing pathology from progress.** Property 6 says models cannot perceive their own deficiencies while executing them. A *system of models* has the same blindness in aggregate. The brief's validation criterion ("restores the diagnostic capacity Selvedge lost around Session 58") is unfalsifiable inside the system. Only the operator can diagnose it — which means the human-review role is doing the work the multi-agent shape is credited with.

### The database substrate is a bet — and the brief misnames it

The brief says structured state should live in a database "with integrity guarantees" and counters should be "derived, not narrated." Agreed in the abstract. But the move from "Markdown is the wrong substrate for state" to "a database is the right one" elides the design question that actually matters: *what is structured state in a methodology for designing complex systems?*

Identifiers and counters are easy. Decisions are not. A decision body is a paragraph of reasoning; "rejected alternatives with reasons" is more paragraphs. If the database stores `decisions(id, body_md, rejected_alternatives_md)`, then 90% of the load-bearing content is back in prose and the database has bought us tamper-evident IDs and a foreign key to `session_id`. That is not nothing — but it is much less than the brief implies.

The prose-state failure looks like this *after* the substrate change: queries return tables of identifiers whose `body_md` columns contradict each other, and no validator can detect this without reading prose, and no model under load reads prose carefully (Property 4). The failure mode reappears as **prose-in-cells** rather than prose-in-files. The brief acknowledges this risk in one sentence ("queries-as-prose form") and does not design against it.

A successor that is honest about this would either (a) commit to a much narrower database — IDs, FKs, lifecycle states, supersession edges, *no body content* — and keep all body content in Markdown files, with the database as an index over the files; or (b) commit to a much more aggressive structuring of decisions themselves (decisions as constrained tuples, not paragraphs), at the cost of the readability the methodology values. The brief commits to neither and leaves the design space ambiguous.

### The subtraction role may be unimplementable as an LLM

The brief asserts a subtraction role is "necessary; not optional." I agree it is necessary. I doubt it can be an LLM agent. Removal of artefacts that stakeholders are attached to — specifications, prior decisions, agents — requires authority the model does not have and judgment the model has not been observed to wield consistently. An LLM asked to subtract will subtract the easy targets (typos, redundant sections) and preserve the load-bearing accretion (because the load-bearing accretion is what trained the agent's sense of what the methodology *is*). A subtraction agent will produce subtraction-ceremony: a periodic review that recommends mostly cosmetic deletions and ratifies the structural accumulation.

Real subtraction is the operator's. The deliberately-built human review role and the subtraction role are likely the same role, and pretending they are two roles dilutes both.

## Where you would not commit

- **Whether the database is net-positive.** If body content stays in Markdown and the database is an index, the bet is small and likely good. If body content moves into the database, I expect prose-in-cells failure within twenty sessions of an active application. I cannot tell from the brief which version is intended. Evidence that would change my mind: a concrete schema where decision bodies, deliberation syntheses, and rejection reasons are *not* free-text columns, plus a worked example showing the methodology can still produce the artefacts it produces today.
- **Whether seven agents is decomposition or proliferation.** The brief's seven roles look reasonable on paper, but I have no evidence the role boundaries hold under real workload pressure. Evidence that would change my mind: a single end-to-end trace of a real session showing each agent's input context, output, and the integration step, with the orchestrator's context measured at every step.
- **Whether self-hosting is a recoverable property.** I argued above that self-hosting may be the structural disease. I am genuinely uncertain. Evidence that would change my mind: an external application of the successor engine (not self-development) that runs for thirty sessions without the operator surfacing structural problems. The methodology must earn its self-hosting privilege by working on something that is not itself first.

## What you think the other perspectives will miss

The architect-perspective and the substrate-perspective will both be drawn into specifying the multi-agent shape and the database schema in detail, because that is where the design satisfaction is. They will under-engage with the question of whether the subtraction role can actually subtract and whether self-hosting is the underlying problem rather than single-agent. The cross-family perspective may surface the right question (it is the structural role for surfacing question-shape) but its answer will be diluted into a "yes-and" if the synthesis tries to integrate all four positions into a coherent design space rather than preserving the disagreement that the diagnosis itself is contested. The blindspot the deliberation is most likely to share: treating "multi-agent + database" as the design space rather than as one candidate region within a larger space that also contains "single-agent + hard write-budget + mandatory external application + human-as-subtractor." That second region is not obviously worse, and it deserves a perspective in the synthesis even if no perspective in this deliberation argues it.
